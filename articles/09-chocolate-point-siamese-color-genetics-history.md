# Chocolate point Siamese: color, genetics, and history
*Milk-chocolate points on an ivory body — and not a dilution of seal, despite what you’ve heard.*
> **Quick answer.** A Siamese chocolate point has a warm ivory body and milk-chocolate brown points — face, ears, paws, and tail. The color comes from a recessive allele (the b allele) at the B locus, which shifts black eumelanin to brown. Chocolate is genetically distinct from seal point, not a lighter version of it. CFA recognized chocolate point Siamese in 1986.

## What does a chocolate point Siamese look like?
The body is ivory — not the cooler, pale fawn of a seal point, but a distinctly warm cream. Think warm white rather than cool white. In certain light, it reads almost buttery.
The points — face mask, ears, legs, and tail — are milk-chocolate brown. Not seal brown, not near-black. The color is warm and medium in tone, closer to a plain chocolate bar than dark chocolate. Nose leather ranges from cinnamon-pink to milk-chocolate. Paw pads are cinnamon-pink. Eyes are the same vivid sapphire blue shared by all classic Siamese point colors.
The practical question most people have: how do I tell a chocolate point from a seal point? Two things to check. First, the points themselves: seal points are near-black, almost dark enough to read as black in shadow. Chocolate points are clearly brown, warm and mid-toned. Second, the body: a seal point’s body has a cooler ivory with a faint grayish cast; a chocolate point’s body is warmer and creamier. Looking at one cat in isolation, focus on the nose leather — seal is dark brown to black, chocolate is pinkish-cinnamon to milk-chocolate.

## Is a chocolate point Siamese just a lighter seal point?
No. This is the most common misconception about the chocolate point, and it’s worth addressing directly.
Seal and chocolate are produced by two different alleles at the same gene locus — the B locus, which controls the production of eumelanin (black-brown pigment). The dominant allele (*B*) produces black eumelanin, which gives seal points their near-black coloring. The recessive allele (*b*) produces brown eumelanin — a structurally different pigment molecule — which gives chocolate points their warm brown color.[2][5]
To be a chocolate point, a cat must inherit two copies of the *b* allele, one from each parent. This is because *b* is fully recessive: a cat with one *B* and one *b* allele (written B/b) will look like a seal point, but carry the chocolate allele silently. Only a B/b parent bred to another B/b or b/b parent can produce chocolate offspring.[7]
The dilution gene is a separate system entirely. That gene (at the MLPH locus) dilutes whichever base pigment is present. Diluting seal produces blue point. Diluting chocolate produces lilac point. So there are really two axes at play: the B locus (black vs brown) and the D locus (full vs diluted). The four classic Siamese colors are the four combinations of those two axes.
A chocolate point is not a faded seal. It’s a cat that produces a different pigment molecule at the cellular level.

## When did CFA recognize the chocolate point Siamese?
Later than you’d expect. The Cat Fanciers’ Association recognized the Siamese as a breed in 1906, with seal point as the founding color.[3] Blue point followed in 1934.[3] But chocolate point did not achieve CFA recognition until 1986 — eighty years after the breed was first registered.[3]
The delay wasn’t about rarity. Breeders had been producing chocolate point Siamese for decades before formal recognition. The delay reflects how long it took registries to formally classify the color as a distinct and stable category rather than a variation or mutation of the seal standard.
In the United Kingdom, the Governing Council of the Cat Fancy recognized a color called “Chestnut Brown” Siamese earlier — the breed standard eventually aligned with “Chocolate” as the internationally accepted term.[6] The divergence in naming caused some confusion in the mid-20th century, with cats exhibited under different names in different countries while being genetically identical.
TICA and most international registries now recognize all four classic point colors.[4]

## How does the chocolate point color develop?
Chocolate point kittens, like all Siamese, are born white. The temperature-sensitive mechanism that restricts pigmentation to the cooler extremities works the same way regardless of point color — the womb provides uniform warmth, and newborns emerge without visible points.[1]
In the first one to two weeks, faint warm-brown ghosting appears on the ears and the edges of the face. The color is subtle at this stage, and harder to detect than in seal kittens, because the lighter pigment offers less contrast against a pale body. By four to six weeks, the chocolate points are clearly distinct. Full adult color sets somewhere between 12 and 18 months — slightly later, on average, than seal.
Chocolate points can deepen slightly with age, following the same mechanism that affects all Siamese: as a cat’s body temperature drops incrementally over the years, the zone where tyrosinase remains active expands. The effect is more muted than in seal points, where the darkening is more visible against an already-dark base.

## Frequently asked questions
**How do I tell a chocolate point from a seal point?**
Check the nose leather first. On a seal point, it’s dark brown to near-black. On a chocolate point, it’s cinnamon-pink or warm milk-chocolate. Then look at the body: seal’s ivory has a cooler cast; chocolate’s ivory is warmer and creamier. The point color itself — near-black vs warm brown — is the clearest tell when you can see both cats together.
**Are chocolate point Siamese rare?**
Rarer than seal and blue, yes. Producing a chocolate point requires both parents to carry the recessive *b* allele at the B locus. Two seal points that don’t carry the *b* allele cannot produce chocolate kittens. The color’s rarity is a function of genetics, not a deliberate breeding choice.
**Can two seal point parents have a chocolate point kitten?**
Yes, if both parents are carriers of the *b* allele (genotype B/b). Two carrier seal points have a 25% chance of producing a b/b (chocolate) kitten in any given litter. If neither parent carries *b*, chocolate offspring are impossible.
**What does CFA say about the chocolate point standard?**
The CFA breed standard describes the chocolate point body as ivory, with no shading. Points should be a warm milk-chocolate brown, with nose leather and paw pads cinnamon-pink to milk-chocolate. Eye color is a deep vivid blue. The standard was formally adopted in 1986 when CFA recognized the chocolate point as a distinct accepted color.[3]
