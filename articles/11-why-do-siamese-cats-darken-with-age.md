# Why do Siamese cats darken with age?
*It comes down to a single enzyme that shuts off when the body gets too warm**,** and slowly reactivates as the cat cools with age.*
> **Quick answer.** Siamese cats darken with age because their pigment-producing enzyme, tyrosinase, is temperature-sensitive, it only activates in body zones cooler than about 33 °C (91 °F). As a cat ages, its body surface temperature gradually falls, allowing the enzyme to produce pigment in areas that stayed pale during kittenhood. The result is progressively darker points and a deepening body color across the cat’s lifetime.

## What is the enzyme that controls Siamese color?
The short answer is tyrosinase. The longer one starts with genetics.
Siamese cats carry a variant of the *TYR* gene called the Himalayan allele (written *cˢ* in most genetics texts). This gene codes for tyrosinase, the enzyme that converts the amino acid tyrosine into melanin, the pigment that makes hair dark.[1] In most cat breeds, tyrosinase works at ordinary body temperature and deposits pigment across the entire coat.
In Siamese cats, the Himalayan allele produces a version of tyrosinase that is thermolabile, meaning it loses its functional shape when exposed to heat. At temperatures above approximately 33 °C (91 °F), the enzyme stops working and no melanin is produced. Below that threshold, it activates and pigment forms.[1][2]
A healthy Siamese has a body core temperature of 38–39 °C, well above the cutoff. The torso, flanks, and back stay pale because the enzyme is inactive there. The extremities, ears, paws, face mask, tail, sit a few degrees cooler, dropping below 33 °C, and so the enzyme activates, melanin accumulates, and the points develop their characteristic color.
The depth of that color is also determined by which point color variety the cat carries. A seal point produces dense near-black pigment in those cooler zones; a lilac point produces a pale pinkish-grey.

## Why are Siamese kittens born white?
The womb provides no opportunity for point development because the temperature is uniform and high, approximately 38–39 °C throughout, which keeps tyrosinase inactive across the entire body.[1] A kitten destined to be a dark seal point looks identical at birth to one that will be a pale lilac point. Both are born pale cream to white.
Within the first week of life, the ears and face begin to cool as the kitten is exposed to room air. The enzyme activates in those areas and pigmentation begins. By week four, distinct point markings are visible. By twelve weeks the pattern is clearly established, though full adult color takes until around nine to twelve months.
This birth-color effect also explains why kittens of two deeply colored Siamese parents can still look almost white for their first few days, the color potential was always there, encoded in the genome. The enzyme just needed room temperature to switch on.

## Why do Siamese cats keep darkening throughout their lives?
The body’s surface temperature doesn’t stay constant across a lifetime.
As a cat ages, changes in body composition, reduced subcutaneous fat, changes in circulation, lower resting metabolic rate, cause the skin surface to cool slightly. Areas that were previously warm enough to keep tyrosinase inactive gradually drop below the 33 °C threshold. The enzyme activates in those areas and pigmentation begins or deepens.[2][3]
The effect is cumulative and slow. A seal point at age two has vivid, dense dark points and an ivory body. The same cat at ten will typically have darker points and a body that has shifted toward fawn or even light tan. The points themselves may spread slightly beyond their original boundaries.
Cold climates accelerate the process. A Siamese moved from a warm country to a northern climate will often show noticeably darker points within a season or two, the body’s extremities are spending more time below the enzyme threshold.[3]
Illness can also trigger temporary darkening. A cat running a fever suppresses melanin production (core temperature rises, more of the body exceeds 33 °C); once the fever resolves and normal surface temperatures return, previous levels of darkening resume.

## What can make a Siamese lighter?
Several things can reduce point depth or slow darkening.
**Warm climate. **A Siamese living in consistently warm temperatures, a heated home, a tropical country, keeps more of its body surface above the enzyme threshold. Points may remain lighter than they would on the same cat in a cooler environment.
**High fever or prolonged illness. **An elevated core temperature suppresses pigmentation across more of the body. The effect is temporary; point color returns once the cat recovers. This shouldn’t be managed as a cosmetic concern, a cat running a fever needs veterinary attention, not a warm room.
**The clipper effect. **When a Siamese’s fur is shaved, for surgery, for example, the regrowth initially comes in lighter. The shaved skin is briefly warmer than normal (without the insulating fur layer), which suppresses tyrosinase. As the fur regrows and the skin cools back to its resting temperature, the new growth gradually darkens to match the surrounding coat. This is well-documented in veterinary practice.[4]
**Certain medications. **Some drugs that affect the tyrosine metabolic pathway or cause systemic changes in body temperature regulation have been associated anecdotally with coat lightening in Siamese. Specific medications should be discussed with a veterinarian before drawing any conclusions.

## FAQ
**Does cold weather make Siamese points darker?**
Yes, over time. A Siamese in a cold climate will typically develop darker points than the same cat in a warm climate, because lower ambient temperature keeps more of the body’s surface below the 33 °C enzyme threshold. A single cold winter won’t produce a dramatic change, but the effect is cumulative across seasons.
**Will my Siamese’s body color stay cream?**
Probably not permanently. Most Siamese bodies shift from ivory toward a warmer cream or fawn as the cat ages, particularly after age five or six. The change is gradual and doesn’t affect the cat’s health. Cats in warmer environments or with higher natural body temperatures tend to retain paler body color longer.
**Is sudden darkening a sign of illness?**
Sudden or rapid changes in coat color are worth noting, but darkening is usually gradual and benign. A sudden change, especially accompanied by other symptoms, is reason to see a vet. Normal aging-related darkening happens slowly over months and years, not overnight.
**Why did my Siamese’s fur grow back lighter after surgery?**
This is the clipper effect described above. When fur is shaved, the uninsulated skin warms slightly, which temporarily suppresses tyrosinase. The regrowth starts lighter and gradually darkens back toward the original shade as the fur returns and normal skin temperatures resume. It typically resolves within a few months.
