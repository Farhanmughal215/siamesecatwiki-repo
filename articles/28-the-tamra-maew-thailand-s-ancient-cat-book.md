# The Tamra Maew: Thailand's ancient cat book
*Thailand's Cat-Book Poems are divinatory manuscripts, not breed registries — but they give us our earliest written description of the Siamese cat.*
> **Quick answer.** The Tamra Maew (ตำราแมว, 'cat treatise' or 'cat-book poems') is a collection of Thai manuscripts from the Ayutthaya period describing cats by their markings and predicting the fortune or misfortune each will bring its owner. Among the cats described as auspicious is the Wichianmat, a pale-bodied, dark-pointed cat that corresponds closely to what we now call the Siamese.

## What is the Tamra Maew Thai cat book?
The Thai name breaks down plainly: *tamra* (ตำรา) means treatise or manual; *maew* (แมว) means cat. 'Cat-book poems' is the translation used by most English-language scholars.
The Tamra Maew is not a single document. It is a collection of manuscripts, different copies, different periods, somewhat different contents, that share the same structure: each entry describes a cat by its physical markings, then assigns that cat a divinatory meaning for its owner. Some cats are auspicious, associated with wealth, protection, or longevity. Others predict illness, poverty, or misfortune.
The form places the Tamra Maew firmly within Thai court manuscript culture. Comparable texts describe auspicious elephants, auspicious horses, propitious calendar days, and favorable omens. The logic is consistent: the physical characteristics of an animal can be read as signs, and interpreting those signs correctly allows a noble household to act accordingly.[1]
The Tamra Maew is therefore a divinatory text, not a breed standard and not a natural history. It tells us what kinds of cats Thai court culture considered meaningful. It cannot tell us when specific breeds were developed, by whom, or how deliberately.

## When was the Tamra Maew manuscript written?
This is where honest uncertainty matters.
The Tamra Maew texts are generally attributed to the Ayutthaya period (1351 to 1767), based on linguistic analysis and cultural context.[2] The Ayutthayan royal court maintained elaborate systems of ritual classification; the Tamra Maew fits this context closely.
However, most surviving manuscripts are not Ayutthayan originals. They are copies made during the early Rattanakosin period, after 1782, when Bangkok replaced the destroyed Ayutthayan capital. Several copies are held at the Bangkok National Library. Whether those copies faithfully reproduce earlier texts, and how much earlier those texts were, remains an open question.[3]
The honest position: the Tamra Maew likely reflects cat-keeping culture from Ayutthayan Thailand, possibly spanning several centuries. The surviving manuscripts are Rattanakosin-era copies of earlier material. No scholarly consensus has firmly closed that gap.

## Siamese cat ancient history: what the Tamra Maew says about the Wichianmat
The Wichianmat (วิเชียรมาศ) is the cat in the Tamra Maew that corresponds most closely to what Western breeders named the Siamese. The word is typically translated as 'moon diamond' or 'white gem,' though the exact shade of meaning is debated.[4]
The Tamra Maew describes the Wichianmat with a pale or white body, dark coloring at the face, ears, legs, and tail, and eyes described as deep or luminous. The cat is listed among the auspicious varieties, associated with good fortune, wealth, and protection for its owner.

The alignment is close enough to be convincing. The pale body and dark points are the defining features of the pointed coat pattern, caused by a temperature-sensitive mutation in the *TYR* gene. The Tamra Maew's authors did not know the genetic mechanism, but they identified and recorded the pattern consistently enough that it can be recognized across centuries.
The connection to the modern seal point Siamese is straightforward: the Wichianmat as described is functionally identical in appearance to a seal point.

## What other cats appear in the Thai cat book?
The Wichianmat is one of several cats described in the manuscripts. The number varies by version; some scholars cite 17 cats, others count more depending on which manuscript copy is being referenced.[4]
Among the cats described alongside the Wichianmat are breeds still recognized in Thailand today. The Korat, a solid silver-blue cat, appears in the manuscripts and is listed as auspicious. The Khao Manee, a white cat with odd-colored eyes, also appears. The Suphalak, a copper-toned cat, is listed as particularly auspicious for royalty.
Not all cats in the manuscripts are lucky. Several varieties are described with inauspicious markings, specific color patterns on the tail, head, or feet, that the text associates with bad fortune or illness. The Tamra Maew covers auspicious, inauspicious, and neutral cats within the same framework.

## What the Tamra Maew cannot tell us
The Tamra Maew is a remarkable document. It is also a limited one, and treating it as more than it is produces historical distortions.
It is a divinatory text, not an empirical record. It tells us what physical features were considered meaningful in Thai court culture; it does not tell us when cats with those features first appeared or whether they were deliberately bred for those traits.
The surviving manuscripts are copies of uncertain age. The Ayutthayan attribution is plausible but not definitively established by any single dated primary document available in published form.
No complete English translation of the Tamra Maew has been formally published as of 2026. English-language accounts depend on partial translations and secondary sources, primarily Martin R. Clutterbuck's *Siamese Cats: Legends and Reality* (2004).[2]
A cat matching the Wichianmat description would not have been unique to Thailand. Temperature-sensitive coat patterns occur naturally across cat populations in cooler climates. The Tamra Maew records that Thai court culture valued and classified this cat; it cannot establish that the cat was exclusive to or first developed in Thailand.

## How the Tamra Maew reached Western cat history
The manuscripts entered Western breed history through a circuitous route. Early Western accounts of the Siamese cat from the 1880s onward mention Thai royal origins and palace cats, but rarely cite primary Thai documents. The Tamra Maew was referenced in Western breed literature primarily through secondary sources, travel accounts, and diplomatic correspondence rather than any direct translation.
It is genuine evidence that pale-bodied, dark-pointed cats were known and valued in Ayutthayan Thailand. It is not evidence of a formal breeding program or a single geographic origin.

## FAQ
**Is the Tamra Maew the oldest cat book in the world?**
It is among the oldest known documents dedicated primarily to describing cats, but 'oldest' is difficult to establish with precision given the dating uncertainties. No comparable cat-specific manuscript of earlier confirmed date has been identified in any other culture.
**Can I read the Tamra Maew in English?**
No complete published English translation exists as of 2026. Partial descriptions and translated excerpts appear in Martin R. Clutterbuck's *Siamese Cats: Legends and Reality* (2004) and in scattered academic papers.
**How many cats are described in the Tamra Maew?**
The number varies by manuscript version. Seventeen is the figure cited most often in English-language secondary sources, but different copies include different entries.
