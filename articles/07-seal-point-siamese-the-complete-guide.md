# Seal point Siamese: the complete guide
*The darkest of the four classic point colors, and the one that defined the breed’s silhouette for over a century.*
> **Quick answer.** A seal point Siamese has an ivory to pale fawn body and near-black (seal brown) extremities, face mask, ears, paws, and tail. The color comes from a temperature-sensitive form of the enzyme tyrosinase, which only produces pigment in the cat’s cooler body zones. Seal is the darkest and most common of the four classic Siamese point colors.

## What does a seal point Siamese look like?
The body is ivory, sometimes described as pale fawn in older cats, and the contrast with the points is the breed’s most recognizable feature. “Points” is the technical term for the extremities: face mask, ears, lower legs, paws, and tail.
On a seal point, those extremities are a deep seal brown, so dark they read as almost black in dim light. Up close, there’s warmth to the color: it sits closer to a very dark espresso than to a true blue-black. The nose leather and paw pads match, dense seal brown to near-black. The eyes are vivid blue, a consequence of the same pigment-limiting genetics that creates the point pattern.[1]
Body color on a seal point is rarely pure white. Even young adults carry a faint warm cast, the CFA breed standard describes the body as “pale fawn to cream, shading gradually into lighter color on the stomach and chest.”[2] The shading matters. A cat with a stark white body and near-black points reads as visually cleaner; a seal point’s ivory-to-fawn body creates a warmer, richer overall impression.
Compared to the other classic points: a chocolate point Siamese has milk-chocolate brown extremities, considerably lighter than seal. A blue point Siamese has slate-grey points on a near-white body. A lilac point has pinkish-grey points on a glacial white body. Seal is the darkest of the four, visually and genetically, it carries the most eumelanin (black pigment) of any classic Siamese variety.

## What causes the seal point color?
The color is controlled by a gene called *TYR*, which encodes an enzyme called tyrosinase. Tyrosinase converts the amino acid tyrosine into melanin, the pigment responsible for dark coloring in skin, hair, and eyes across mammals.[3]
In most cats, tyrosinase works at normal body temperature and produces pigment everywhere. In a Siamese, the *TYR* gene carries what geneticists call the Himalayan allele (written *cˢ* or *cʰ* depending on the notation system). This variant produces a version of tyrosinase that is temperature-sensitive: it denatures, loses its shape and stops working, at temperatures above approximately 33 °C (91 °F).[3][4]
A healthy cat’s body core runs at 38–39 °C. The enzyme is inactive there. No pigment forms on the torso, flanks, or top of the head. But the extremities, ears, paws, face, tip of the tail, are a few degrees cooler, sitting below the threshold. The enzyme activates, pigment forms, and the points develop their color.
The depth of that color depends on which specific allele a cat carries. The seal point pattern results from a cat that is homozygous for the Himalayan allele (*cˢ cˢ*) and carries two copies of the dense (non-dilute) pigment gene. Seal is full-density eumelanin expressed only where the body is cool enough to allow it.[4]
This is why Siamese cats darken across their lifetimes and in cold climates, the body’s surface temperature falls incrementally with age and environment, allowing pigment to form in areas that were previously warm enough to stay pale.

## How does the color develop from kitten to adult?
A seal point kitten is born white. Every kitten is, because the womb is uniformly warm at roughly 38–39 °C, which keeps tyrosinase inactive everywhere. There are no points at birth, regardless of what color the cat will become.[3]
Within the first week, the ears and face begin to cool below 33 °C as the kitten is exposed to room temperature air. A faint darkening, sometimes described as a shadowing or ghosting, appears at the ear tips and around the nose. By week four, distinct points are visible. By twelve weeks, the pattern is clearly established.
Full adult color takes longer. Most seal points reach their mature point color somewhere between nine and twelve months of age. By that point the seal brown is dense and the contrast with the body is sharp. But the process doesn’t stop there.
The body color, ivory in a young adult, gradually warms and deepens across the cat’s life. Senior seal points often develop a noticeably fawn or even light brown cast to the body, and the points themselves continue to darken. This isn’t a health concern; it’s the same enzyme at work, responding to slight decreases in surface temperature as the cat ages.

## How does seal point compare to the other three classic colors?
There are four colors recognized as “classic” by the Cat Fanciers’ Association: seal, chocolate, blue, and lilac. They form two pairs. Seal and chocolate are the dense (full-pigment) versions; blue and lilac are their dilute counterparts. A single copy of the dilute gene (*d*) doesn’t change the color; two copies do.[2][5]

The genetic relationship: blue is a dilute seal (same eumelanin, half the density). Lilac is a dilute chocolate. A cat carrying two copies of the dilute gene (*d/d*) converts seal to blue and chocolate to lilac.
In practice, distinguishing a dark chocolate point from a young seal point can be difficult before full maturity. The clearest diagnostic is nose leather color: seal points have near-black to dark brown nose leather; chocolate points have a distinctly pinkish or cinnamon-toned nose leather even in adulthood.

## Which famous cats were seal points?
Three of the most recognizable Siamese cats in film history were seal points, or were depicted with seal point coloring.
**Si and Am** (*Lady and the Tramp*, Disney, 1955) are the villainous Siamese duo whose “We Are Siamese” number became one of the studio’s most remembered sequences. Their dark masks and near-black points match seal point coloring closely, though Disney’s production notes don’t appear to specify the variety by name.[6]
**Pyewacket** (*Bell, Book and Candle*, Columbia Pictures, 1958) is the familiar of witch Gillian Holroyd, played by Kim Novak. The cat cast in the role was a working Siamese actor whose dark points read as seal in the film’s photography and early color prints. The name Pyewacket, borrowed from a 17th-century English witch-trial record, has since become a popular name for dark Siamese cats.[7]
**DC** (*That Darn Cat!*, Disney, 1965), short for Darn Cat, is a street-savvy Siamese who becomes the unwitting lead in an FBI kidnapping case. The real cat used in filming, a seal point named Syn, was trained by Hollywood animal trainer Frank Inn. DC’s seal coloring is central to several plot scenes involving identification.[8]

## FAQ
**Is seal point the same as “classic” or “traditional” Siamese?**
“Seal point” describes a color; “traditional” or “classic” describes a body type. The two are independent. A seal point can be either a modern (wedge-headed) or traditional (apple-headed) Siamese. Most early photographs of Siamese cats show seal points because it was historically the most prevalent color, but the terms aren’t interchangeable.
**Do seal points get darker as they age?**
Yes. The same enzyme that creates the point pattern, tyrosinase, responds to cooler temperatures, and a cat’s body surface cools slightly with age. The process is gradual and lifelong. A seal point at ten years will typically be darker overall than the same cat at two years.
**Are seal points rare?**
Seal is the most common of the four classic Siamese point colors, not a rare variety. The seal allele is ancestrally dominant in the breed’s gene pool. If you’re looking at a Siamese without knowing its color, there’s a reasonable chance it’s a seal point.
**What does the CFA breed standard say about seal point coloring?**
The CFA standard specifies that the body be pale fawn to cream, shading gradually to lighter on the stomach and chest. The points, mask, ears, legs, feet, and tail, should be a consistent, dense seal brown, clearly defined and showing strong contrast with the body. Any barring (striping) in the points is considered a fault.[2]
