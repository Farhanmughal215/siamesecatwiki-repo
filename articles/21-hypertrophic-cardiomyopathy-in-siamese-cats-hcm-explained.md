# Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy in Siamese cats: HCM explained
*HCM is the most common heart disease in cats. Siamese are among the breeds where it appears more often, and knowing the warning signs can save a life.*
> **Quick answer.** HCM (hypertrophic cardiomyopathy) in Siamese cats is a condition where the walls of the heart's left ventricle thicken, reducing its ability to fill and pump blood. Siamese are among the breeds with elevated risk, though the genetic cause is less well defined than in Maine Coons. Symptoms range from none at all to labored breathing, collapse, and sudden cardiac death. Early detection through echocardiogram is the best tool owners and vets have.

## What is HCM in cats?
Cardiomyopathy means disease of the heart muscle (myocardium). Hypertrophic describes what happens to that muscle: it thickens. Specifically, the walls of the left ventricle, the chamber that pumps oxygenated blood out to the body, become abnormally thick.
The problem is not that the heart is getting stronger. A thickened wall is stiffer. A stiff wall cannot relax properly between beats, so the ventricle fills with less blood than it should. Less blood in means less blood out. Over time, that reduced output strains the rest of the heart and the lungs.
In severe cases, the reduced chamber volume and increased pressure cause fluid to back up into the lungs, a condition called congestive heart failure (CHF). Some cats with HCM also develop blood clots in the heart's left atrium. These can break loose and travel to the aorta, blocking blood flow to the hindlimbs. That event, an aortic thromboembolism (sometimes called a saddle thrombus), is one of the most serious complications of feline heart disease.[1]
HCM is the most commonly diagnosed cardiac disease in cats overall, affecting an estimated 15% of cats in some echocardiographic surveys.[2] It occurs across many breeds and in domestic shorthairs, but certain breeds show higher rates. Siamese are among them.

## Are Siamese cats at higher risk for HCM?
Siamese cats are considered a breed with elevated HCM risk, though the picture is more complicated than it is for some other breeds.
In Maine Coons and Ragdolls, researchers have identified specific mutations in the MYBPC3 gene, the same gene implicated in human familial HCM, that strongly predict disease.[3] A cat carrying two copies of the Maine Coon MYBPC3 variant has a very high probability of developing HCM. Genetic tests exist for those breeds.
Siamese are different. The breed has a documented predisposition, but no single causative mutation has been consistently identified and validated across the Siamese population as of this writing. The underlying genetics appear to be more complex, possibly polygenic (involving multiple genes), or they include variants that have not yet been characterized in published research.[4]
What this means practically: there is no off-the-shelf genetic test that clears a Siamese of HCM risk the way breeders can screen Maine Coons. Cardiac evaluation remains the primary screening tool for Siamese cat heart disease.
## Siamese cat HCM symptoms: what to watch for
One of HCM's most difficult features is that many cats show no symptoms at all in the early stages. The heart compensates for a long time before signs appear. When they do, the range is wide.
**Symptoms that warrant a vet visit:**
- ***Reduced activity or reluctance to jump***
- ***Faster-than-normal resting respiratory rate (over 30 breaths per minute at rest is a common monitoring threshold)***
- ***Soft cough (uncommon in cats; if present, investigate promptly)***
- ***Weight loss without obvious cause***
- ***Heart murmur detected at a routine exam***

**Symptoms that are emergencies: go to a vet immediately.**
- ***Labored breathing or rapid, shallow breaths***
- ***Open-mouth breathing (cats almost never breathe through their mouths unless in serious distress)***
- ***Sudden collapse or inability to use the hindlimbs***
- ***Extreme lethargy combined with any breathing change***
- ***Blue or pale gums***

Open-mouth breathing and labored breathing in a cat are never normal. They indicate the lungs may be filling with fluid or oxygen levels have dropped critically. Treat either as an emergency.

## How is HCM diagnosed in Siamese cats?
### Echocardiogram
The echocardiogram (cardiac ultrasound) is the gold standard for diagnosing HCM in Siamese cats. It measures the exact thickness of the ventricular walls and the septum, assesses chamber size, and evaluates blood flow in real time. A left ventricular wall thicker than 6 mm in diastole (when the heart is relaxed) is generally considered diagnostic in cats, though cardiologists evaluate the full picture, not a single number.[1]
Most general practitioners can perform a basic echo. A board-certified veterinary cardiologist provides the most detailed interpretation.
### Auscultation
Listening to the heart with a stethoscope can detect a murmur, a sound caused by turbulent blood flow. Heart murmurs are graded on a scale of 1 to 6 (Grade 1 is barely audible; Grade 6 is audible without a stethoscope). Many cats with HCM have a murmur, but the relationship is imperfect: some HCM cats have no murmur, and some murmurs occur without HCM.[5] A murmur is a reason to pursue an echocardiogram, not a diagnosis by itself.
### Chest X-ray
A chest radiograph shows the overall heart size and, critically, whether there is fluid in or around the lungs. This is most useful when CHF is suspected. It complements rather than replaces the echo.
### Biomarker blood tests
NT-proBNP (N-terminal pro-brain natriuretic peptide) is a blood test that measures a hormone released when heart muscle is under stress. Elevated levels can support a cardiac diagnosis and flag cats that may benefit from an echo.[6] It is a screening tool, not a definitive test.

## What is the prognosis for a Siamese cat with HCM?
Prognosis ranges widely depending on how far the disease has progressed at diagnosis.
Cats found to have mild, subclinical HCM (thickened walls but no symptoms and no CHF) may remain stable for months to years with no intervention beyond monitoring. Some never progress to clinical disease during their lifetime.
Cats diagnosed at the CHF stage face a more guarded outlook. Median survival times after a first CHF episode vary across studies, but many cats respond well to treatment and live a year or more with good quality of life.[1]
Negative prognostic indicators include significant enlargement of the left atrium (which increases clot risk), the presence of a thrombus, and poor response to initial medication. Any aortic thromboembolism event carries a serious short-term prognosis, though some cats do survive and regain limb function.
The unpredictable factor is sudden cardiac death, which can occur without prior warning even in cats not known to have advanced disease. This is one reason annual cardiac monitoring is recommended for Siamese cats.

## How is Siamese cat HCM managed?
There is no cure for HCM. Management aims to slow progression, reduce symptom burden, and lower the risk of complications like clot formation and CHF.
Your vet or veterinary cardiologist will tailor the approach to your cat's specific stage. Common categories of medications used in feline HCM management include:[1][7]
- ***Beta-blockers (such as atenolol): ******can slow the heart rate and reduce the force of contraction, giving the stiff ventricle more time to fill***
- ***Calcium channel blockers (such as diltiazem): ******also reduce heart rate and may help with relaxation of the ventricle***
- ***Diuretics (such as furosemide): ******used when fluid has accumulated in or around the lungs; work to remove excess fluid***
- ***Antiplatelet or anticoagulant therapy (such as clopidogrel): ******reduces clot formation in cats with left atrial enlargement***

This article does not recommend specific drugs or dosages. The right protocol depends on your cat's individual presentation. Medication use evolves as guidelines are updated; always follow current veterinary guidance.
If your Siamese cat is diagnosed with HCM, ask your vet about referral to a veterinary cardiologist for a baseline echo and monitoring plan.

## Siamese cat HCM screening recommendations
Because Siamese lack a validated genetic screening test, physical examination and imaging are the primary tools:
- ***Annual cardiac auscultation ******at every routine wellness exam, starting from adulthood. Ask your vet to listen carefully and document findings each year so changes can be tracked over time.***
- ***Echocardiogram for breeding cats. ******Siamese breeders are encouraged to have cats evaluated by a cardiologist before breeding, and to recheck periodically. The ACVIM (American College of Veterinary Internal Medicine) has published consensus guidelines for cardiac screening in breeding cats.[1]***
- ***Monitor resting respiratory rate at home. ******Count breaths per minute while your cat is sleeping. A consistent reading above 30 breaths per minute warrants a vet call. Apps are available to help track this over time.***
- ***Report any sudden changes. ******Behavioral shifts, reduced appetite, or any change in breathing pattern deserve attention sooner rather than later.***

## Siamese cat HCM: frequently asked questions
**Can a Siamese cat with HCM live a normal life?**
Many can, especially when caught early. Cats in the subclinical stage often need no medication and can remain stable for years. Even cats in the early CHF stage frequently do well on treatment. Close monitoring and prompt attention to any symptom changes are the most important factors in long-term quality of life.
**Does a heart murmur mean my Siamese has HCM?**
Not necessarily. Murmurs have several causes, and some cats with HCM have no murmur at all. A murmur is a reason to get an echocardiogram, which will give a clear answer, not a diagnosis on its own.
**Is there a genetic test for HCM in Siamese cats?**
Not a validated one. Unlike Maine Coons, where specific MYBPC3 mutations are identified, the Siamese genetic basis for HCM is not fully characterized. Echocardiography remains the best tool for screening Siamese cats for heart disease.
**How often should my Siamese cat have a cardiac checkup?**
At minimum, annual auscultation at a routine wellness exam. For cats over 7 years old, or any cat with a murmur or family history of cardiac disease, ask your vet whether periodic echocardiography is appropriate.
**What is an aortic thromboembolism and should I be worried about it?**
An aortic thromboembolism (ATE) is when a blood clot (often formed in the enlarged left atrium of an HCM cat) travels to the aorta and blocks blood flow, typically to the hindlimbs. Signs include sudden hindlimb paralysis, cold legs, and extreme distress. It is a medical emergency. Cats at high risk (those with significant left atrial enlargement) are often prescribed antiplatelet therapy by their vet to reduce this risk.
