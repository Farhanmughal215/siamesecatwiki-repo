# How the Siamese cat came to the West
*The breed's Western story begins not at a London cat show, but in a diplomatic package addressed to the White House.*
> **Quick answer.** The first documented Siamese cat in the United States was named Siam, sent to First Lady Lucy Hayes in 1878 by David Sickels, the American consul in Bangkok — six years before the breed's famous British debut. The Crystal Palace exhibition of 1884 or 1885 (sources differ on the exact year) made the Siamese a public sensation in Britain; within two decades, breed clubs and formal standards followed on both sides of the Atlantic.

## The first Siamese cat in America: the White House cat of 1878
The earliest documented Siamese in the United States arrived through the US diplomatic service, not a breeder or a cat show.
In 1878, David Sickels, the American consul stationed in Bangkok, sent a Siamese cat to the White House as a gift for First Lady Lucy Webb Hayes. The cat was named Siam. A letter from Sickels accompanying the shipment survives in White House records and is cited by the White House Historical Association as documentation of the gift.[1] The letter notes that the cat was obtained with some difficulty and was considered a notable specimen.
What the Sickels letter establishes definitively is that a Siamese cat was present in the United States by 1878. This predates the breed's famous British introduction by at least six years, a fact often overlooked in accounts that treat the Crystal Palace exhibition as the beginning of the Western story.

## Britain's introduction: Gould and the Crystal Palace
The British introduction is better documented and more consequential for the breed's subsequent history.
Owen Gould was a British consul general serving in Bangkok. He brought a pair of Siamese cats to Britain, commonly cited as Pho and Mia in secondary sources, and his sister Lilian Jane Gould exhibited them at the Crystal Palace Cat Show.[2] The year is given as 1884 in some sources and 1885 in others; the discrepancy has not been resolved by a single primary-source date that all historians accept.
Harrison Weir, the founder of the organized cat fancy in Britain, described Siamese cats in his 1889 book *Our Cats and All About Them*. Weir wrote with evident surprise about the cats' unusual appearance, the pointed coat, the kinked tail (then common and not yet bred out), the crossed eyes, and the loud, insistent voice.[3]
The press reaction was mixed. A judge reportedly described the Siamese as 'an unnatural nightmare of a cat,' a phrase widely quoted in breed histories, though the primary attribution is unclear and the exact wording varies across sources.[4]
Whether the initial reaction was distaste or fascination, the effect was the same: the Siamese became a talking point. Within a few years of the Crystal Palace appearances, Siamese were appearing regularly at British cat shows and changing hands at prices that reflected their novelty.

## The Siamese Cat Club and early British breed standards
The Siamese Cat Club was founded in Britain in 1902, the first breed-specific cat club in Britain dedicated to the Siamese.[5] Its founding marked a shift from the cat as a curiosity to a subject of deliberate breeding and formal classification.
The Club's early breed standard reflected the cats that had arrived from Thailand in the 1880s: a moderate, muscular animal with a distinctly wedge-shaped head, large ears, and the characteristic pointed coloring. The kinked tail and crossed eyes (strabismus), which had been common in imported cats, were gradually discouraged and eventually disqualified in show competition.[5]
Frances Simpson, in *The Book of the Cat* (1903), documented the early British Siamese breeding community with photographs and breeder profiles. The cats in those photographs are noticeably different from modern show Siamese, broader-headed, shorter-nosed, more robustly built, and much closer to the traditional conformation that TICA now recognizes as the Thai breed.[6]

## How the breed spread through North America and Europe
The Cat Fanciers' Association was founded in the United States in 1906, and the Siamese was among the breeds it recognized from the outset.[7] American breeders had been importing Siamese from Britain through the 1890s and early 1900s; by the time the CFA was organized, a working population of Siamese in the US was already established.
Growth accelerated sharply after the Second World War. By the early 1950s, the Siamese was the most registered cat breed in both Britain and the United States, a position it held through the 1960s and into the 1970s.[7]
It was also in the 1950s and 1960s that American show breeders began selecting for the increasingly extreme wedge-head conformation that defines the modern show type. That divergence is covered in the full history of the Siamese cat.

## Why the British introduction story dominated
The standard account of Siamese history in the West centers on Crystal Palace. The White House cat of 1878 is mentioned, when it is mentioned at all, as a footnote rather than a starting point. The reason is simple: historiography follows documentation.
The Crystal Palace exhibition was a public event with a press audience, organized records, and subsequent coverage in the cat fancy's growing literature. The Sickels gift was a private diplomatic gesture. It generated a letter but no show, no press coverage, and no subsequent breeding program that could be traced back to Siam's arrival.
This is not a correction that diminishes the Crystal Palace moment. It simply places the American arrival where it belongs: six years earlier, quieter, and more significant as a diplomatic curiosity than as a founding moment for the breed.

## FAQ
**Who brought the first Siamese cat to America?**
David Sickels, the American consul in Bangkok, sent a Siamese cat named Siam to First Lady Lucy Webb Hayes in 1878. This is the first documented Siamese in the United States, predating the breed's British public debut by at least six years.
**When were Siamese first shown in Britain?**
Lilian Jane Gould exhibited a pair of Siamese cats at the Crystal Palace Cat Show. The year is given as 1884 in some sources and 1885 in others; breed historians have not settled on a single confirmed date.
**When did the CFA first recognize the Siamese?**
The Cat Fanciers' Association recognized the Siamese in 1906, the year the organization was founded.
**What did early Western Siamese cats look like?**
The cats that arrived in Britain and the US in the 1880s to 1900s were more robustly built than the modern show Siamese, with rounded rather than extreme wedge heads, and often had kinked tails and crossed eyes that were later bred out of Western show lines.
