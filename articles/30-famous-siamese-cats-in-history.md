# Famous Siamese cats in history
*The breed has a paper trail — consular letters, show records, film credits, and one Guinness certificate — and where the paper ends, this article says so.*
> **Quick answer.** Famous Siamese cats include Siam, who arrived at the White House in 1878 as the first documented Siamese in the United States; Pho and Mia, the pair exhibited at the Crystal Palace Cat Show who sparked the breed's popularity in Britain; Pyewacket and DC, Siamese film stars of the 1950s and 1960s; and Scooter, a Texas-born Siamese whose 30-year lifespan earned him a Guinness World Record in 2016.

## Siam: the first famous Siamese cat in America
The Siamese cat arrived in the United States through official diplomatic channels. In 1878, David Sickels, the American consul stationed in Bangkok, sent a Siamese cat to First Lady Lucy Webb Hayes. The cat was named Siam. The gift is documented in Sickels's correspondence, which survives in the Rutherford B. Hayes Presidential Library in Fremont, Ohio.[1]
Sickels's letter describes his effort to obtain the cat, which he calls a rare specimen of the 'royal Siamese' breed. This makes Siam the earliest Siamese cat in the United States for which a primary-source paper trail exists: a named consul, a named recipient, and an archived letter.
What happened to Siam after arrival is less certain. Breed histories state that the cat did not survive long at the White House, though the primary source for that claim is not consistently cited.

## Pho and Mia: the pair that launched the British Siamese craze
In 1884 or 1885, a British consul general named Owen Gould brought a breeding pair of Siamese cats from Bangkok to England. His sister, Lilian Jane Gould, exhibited the cats at the Crystal Palace Cat Show in London. The pair drew attention that shifted the breed's trajectory in Britain entirely.[2]
The Crystal Palace show was judged by Harrison Weir, the father of organized cat showing in England, and the cats were written about by breed historians including Frances Simpson. Both describe the Siamese as an object of considerable curiosity.
The names Pho and Mia are widely reported in Siamese breed histories as the names of Gould's cats.
What is not in dispute: a breeding pair arrived, they were shown at Crystal Palace, and their appearance there generated the wave of Siamese interest in Britain that followed through the late 1880s and 1890s.

## Pyewacket: the famous Siamese film cat in Bell, Book and Candle
*Bell, Book and Candle* (1958) is a romantic comedy in which Kim Novak plays a modern Manhattan witch. Her Siamese familiar, the one whose behavior exposes her to Jimmy Stewart's character, is named Pyewacket. The name is not invented for the film.
Matthew Hopkins, the self-styled Witch Finder General of 17th-century England, recorded 'Pyewacket' in 1647 as the name of a familiar spirit allegedly confessed to during the Essex witch trials. The name predates the film by three centuries.[3]
The Siamese cat who played Pyewacket was trained for the role. Siamese were, and remain, among the more trainable domestic cat breeds, which made them a practical choice for studio animal trainers of the era.

## DC: the Siamese cat detective in That Darn Cat!
Disney's *That Darn Cat!* (1965) centers on a roaming seal point Siamese who inadvertently leads the FBI to a kidnapping suspect. The cat's name in the film is DC, short for Darn Cat. The animal actor who played DC was named Syn Cat, a seal point Siamese trained by the studio's contracted animal handler.[4]
Syn Cat's performance required a cat to carry a watch on its collar, move on cue through outdoor locations, and interact naturally with a large cast. For Siamese cat owners, DC's intelligence and persistent nosiness in the film reads as characteristically accurate to the breed.

## Scooter: the oldest Siamese cat on record
Scooter was a male Siamese cat born on March 26, 1986, in Mansfield, Texas. He died in 2016 at the age of 30. Guinness World Records certified him as the world's oldest living cat at the time of the record's verification.[5]
His owner, Gail Floyd, attributed his longevity to consistent veterinary care, an active daily routine, and extensive travel. At 30 he is also the oldest Siamese on record in documented sources.
For context: the all-time domestic cat longevity record belongs to Creme Puff, a mixed-breed female from Austin, Texas, verified by Guinness at 38 years and 3 days. Scooter at 30 is exceptional but Creme Puff's record stands separately.

## A note on Nora the Piano Cat
Nora was a viral internet cat known from a 2007 YouTube video in which she appears to press piano keys repeatedly. She is frequently misidentified as a Siamese in online breed roundups. Contemporary news coverage and the official account maintained by her owner consistently describe her as a gray domestic shorthair, not a Siamese.
Based on available evidence, Nora was not a Siamese cat. This section exists to prevent the wiki from repeating a common misattribution.

## FAQ
**Which Siamese cat was kept at the White House?**
A cat named Siam, sent to First Lady Lucy Webb Hayes in 1878 by David Sickels, the American consul in Bangkok. The gift is documented in consular correspondence held at the Rutherford B. Hayes Presidential Library.
**What is the oldest Siamese cat ever recorded?**
Scooter, a male Siamese from Mansfield, Texas, who died in 2016 at the age of 30. Guinness World Records certified him as the world's oldest living cat at the time. The all-time oldest domestic cat record belongs to Creme Puff, a mixed-breed cat who lived to 38.
**Which famous Siamese cats appeared in films?**
Two notable Siamese film roles are well documented. Pyewacket appeared in *Bell, Book and Candle* (1958), playing Kim Novak's witch's familiar. DC, played by a trained seal point Siamese named Syn Cat, was the protagonist of Disney's *That Darn Cat!* (1965).
**Is Nora the Piano Cat a Siamese?**
Almost certainly not. Contemporary coverage consistently describes Nora as a gray domestic shorthair. She appears in Siamese roundups online due to misattribution. This wiki does not count her among famous Siamese cats unless new evidence emerges.
