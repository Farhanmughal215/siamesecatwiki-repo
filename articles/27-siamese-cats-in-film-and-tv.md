# Siamese cats in film and TV
*No breed has logged more screen time, or stirred more controversy, than the Siamese.*
> **Quick answer.** Siamese cats have appeared in film since the 1950s, most famously as Si and Am in Disney's Lady and the Tramp (1955) and DC in That Darn Cat! (1965). Their sharp pointed coloring, blue eyes, and distinctive voice made them natural screen performers. The breed's Hollywood peak ran roughly from 1955 to 1970, shaping public perception of Siamese cats for decades.

## Siamese cat Disney: Si and Am in Lady and the Tramp
The most culturally consequential Siamese cats in film history are animated. Si and Am appear in Disney's Lady and the Tramp (1955) for roughly four minutes of screen time. Their scene, set to the 'We Are Siamese' song, became one of the studio's most discussed sequences in the decades that followed.
Peggy Lee wrote and performed 'We Are Siamese' for the film. The song's lyrics, delivered in exaggerated accents that drew on a reductive 'oriental' caricature, and the cats' design drew little notice in 1955. Academic criticism of the sequence became more prominent from the 1990s onward, as scholars examining race and representation in Hollywood animation identified it alongside other sequences in the Disney catalogue.[1]
The criticism is legitimate: the song uses mock-Asian phonics and syntax, and the cats' behavior, scheming, disruptive, alien, reinforces a stereotype that was already well-worn when the film was made.
When Disney remade Lady and the Tramp as a live-action film in 2019, the studio replaced 'We Are Siamese' with a new song performed by Nicki Minaj and Janelle Monae. The cats were redesigned and renamed Devon and Meeko. The original sequence was retained without alteration in the 1955 film's home release; Disney added a content advisory.[2]
Despite the controversy, Si and Am lodged the Siamese firmly in the public imagination. Siamese cat registrations in the US rose noticeably in the years following the film's release.[3]

## Pyewacket: the Siamese cat witch's familiar
*Bell, Book and Candle* (Columbia Pictures, 1958) takes a different approach. Kim Novak plays Gillian Holroyd, a Manhattan witch who owns a Siamese cat named Pyewacket, her familiar and the most important prop in the film. Certain magic only works while the cat is present, and Gillian's eventual loss of her powers is bound up with the cat's departure.
The name Pyewacket is not invented. Matthew Hopkins, the self-appointed Witch-Finder General of 17th-century England, recorded it in his 1647 pamphlet *The Discovery of Witches* as one of the familiar spirits allegedly named by a confessing witch in Essex.[4] The name's archaic sound is presumably why the film's writers chose it.
The Siamese used in the production was a trained cat actor. Whether the cat was a seal point or blue point is difficult to confirm from available color prints; the coloring reads as moderately dark, consistent with seal, but no production record specifying the variety has been located.[5]
Pyewacket's influence outlasted the film. The name became, and remains, a popular choice for Siamese cats. The 2019 horror film Pyewacket takes its title directly from the same historical source, with no Siamese cat involved.

## DC: the Siamese cat who solved a kidnapping
*That Darn Cat!* (Walt Disney Productions, 1965) gives a Siamese the most plot-functional role any cat has played on screen. DC, a seal point Siamese, roams his suburban neighborhood at night. When he returns home wearing a wristwatch belonging to a kidnapping victim, an FBI agent (Dean Jones) has to track DC's movements to find where the hostage is being held.
The real cat in the production was a trained Siamese named Syn, handled by Hollywood animal trainer Frank Inn, who also worked with the dog Benji.[6] Syn's seal point coloring appears several times as a plot detail, since DC is identified by his markings across scenes.
Disney remade the film in 1997 with Christina Ricci, keeping the Siamese lead. The 1965 original remains the clearest example of the Siamese's screen appeal working on a practical level: the cat's markings are memorable, its face is expressive in close-up, and its tendency to move purposefully photographs as intentional rather than random.

## Sagwa and animated Siamese cats in pop culture
Amy Tan published *The Chinese Siamese Cat* in 1994, a picture book following a Siamese kitten named Sagwa living in a Mandarin's household in dynastic China. The story was adapted into an animated series, *Sagwa, the Chinese Siamese Cat*, which ran on PBS Kids from 2001 to 2002 for 40 episodes.[7]
The show is notable for what it isn't: Sagwa is not the scheming, exoticized figure of the Si-and-Am tradition. She is curious, principled, and frequently in conflict with authority. The series was produced with Tan's involvement and aimed explicitly at young children.
Outside these major productions, Tao the Siamese appears in *The Incredible Journey* (1963), traveling with two dogs across the Canadian wilderness in a quieter, more naturalistic portrayal than any Disney production.[8]

## Why Siamese cats became Hollywood's cat of choice
The practical answer: they are legible on camera.
A Siamese has three visually distinctive features, the pointed coat pattern, the blue eyes, and the face mask, that all read clearly in both black-and-white and color film. A grey tabby or solid-white cat blends into backgrounds. A Siamese does not. The dark mask creates a natural framing for the face in close-up; the blue eyes catch light in a way that reads as expressive even on standard camera lenses of the 1950s and 1960s.
Beyond visuals, the Siamese voice is an asset. The breed is genuinely vocal: its calls are louder, longer, and more varied in pitch than most domestic cats. That is useful in a sound-era film when a cat needs to register as present and active without the director cutting away from the human actors.
There is also a cultural logic specific to the period. In the 1950s and 1960s, Siamese cats were fashionable, associated with sophisticated urban ownership. Placing one in a scene was a quick shorthand for a certain kind of household. This is visible in *Bell, Book and Candle*, where Pyewacket functions partly as a status symbol as well as a plot device.
That fashionability faded after the 1970s. Film and television Siamese appearances became less frequent, and the cats that did appear were less often coded as glamorous or threatening. For more on how the breed embedded itself in culture, see the history of the Siamese cat and famous Siamese cats.

## FAQ
**What song do the Siamese cats sing in Lady and the Tramp?**
The song is 'We Are Siamese (If You Please),' written and performed by Peggy Lee. It appears in the 1955 Disney animated film. The 2019 live-action remake replaced it with a new song performed by Nicki Minaj and Janelle Monae.
**What breed of cat played DC in That Darn Cat!?**
DC was played by a trained seal point Siamese named Syn. The cat's trainer was Frank Inn, a Hollywood animal handler also known for working with the dog who played Benji.
**What is Pyewacket in Bell, Book and Candle?**
Pyewacket is the Siamese familiar of the witch Gillian Holroyd, played by Kim Novak. The name Pyewacket comes from a 1647 English pamphlet, *The Discovery of Witches*, by Matthew Hopkins, which lists it among names given to witches' imps.
**Did the Siamese cats in Lady and the Tramp cause the breed's popularity to increase?**
Registration data from the years following the 1955 release suggests a rise in Siamese registrations, consistent with the pattern seen when other breeds receive prominent screen exposure. Whether the film was the direct cause is difficult to establish from aggregate figures alone.
