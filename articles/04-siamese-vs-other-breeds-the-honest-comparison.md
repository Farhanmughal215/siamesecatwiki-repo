# Siamese vs other breeds: the honest comparison
*The Siamese is not the right cat for everyone**;** but for the right person, nothing else comes close. Here’s how it stacks up.*
> **Quick answer.** The Siamese cat vs comparison most often comes down to three questions: how much noise can you live with, how much alone time will the cat have, and how much predictability do you want in a pet? This article compares the Siamese against the Burmese, Balinese, Oriental Shorthair, domestic shorthair, and Tonkinese; with side-by-side tables and an honest editorial take on each pairing.
No breed is universally superior. These comparisons exist to help you match the right cat to your actual lifestyle; not to your idealized version of it. Be honest about how much noise you can handle, how often you’re home, and how much engagement you want from a pet.

## Siamese vs Burmese: similar warmth, different volume
The Burmese is the comparison most Siamese enthusiasts reach for first, and for good reason. Both breeds are intensely people-oriented, highly social, and active well into adulthood. If you’ve ruled out independent breeds and you want a cat that genuinely wants to be with you, both are strong candidates.
The difference is in degree. The Siamese is louder; substantially louder. It uses a low, resonant yowl that carries through walls and does not stop when ignored. The Burmese is vocal, but the sound is softer and the frequency lower. The Burmese is also stockier: a classic “brick wrapped in silk” body type, heavier and more compact than the angular Siamese frame. And the Burmese handles solitude better; not well, but better. A Siamese alone for eight hours every day is a crisis in slow motion; a Burmese in the same situation is unhappy but more resilient.[1][2]
On health: Burmese cats are susceptible to hypokalaemic polymyopathy; a condition involving low blood potassium that can cause episodic muscle weakness, particularly in Burmese lines from Australia and the UK.[6] Siamese carry a higher risk of hepatic amyloidosis and feline asthma. Both breeds benefit from cardiac screening; neither is unusually fragile.

## Siamese vs Balinese: the same cat in a different coat
The Balinese is a natural mutation of the Siamese. In the 1950s, Siamese litters occasionally produced kittens with semi-long, silky coats. Instead of culling them, some breeders preserved and developed that trait. The result is a cat that is, for almost all practical purposes, a long-haired Siamese.[3]
Same pointed coat pattern. Same vivid blue eyes. Same angular body type. Same vocal, intensely social temperament. The Balinese yowls. It follows you. It has opinions. It does not enjoy being left alone. The personality profile is nearly identical to the Siamese, and the health profile is similarly close; the same genetic background means the same elevated risks for amyloidosis and HCM.
The meaningful differences are two. First, the coat: the Balinese has a semi-long, single-layer silky coat that lies flat rather than puffing out. It sheds less than you’d expect for a long-haired breed; considerably less than a Persian or a Maine Coon; but it does require more grooming than a Siamese (two to three times per week rather than once). Second, the Balinese is slightly rarer than the Siamese, which can mean longer wait times for kittens from reputable breeders.

## Siamese vs Oriental Shorthair: same bones, every color
The Oriental Shorthair is the Siamese without the pointed restriction. It shares the same foreign body type; long, lean, angular, fine-boned; and the same vocal, socially demanding personality. The difference is the coat: where a Siamese can only be pointed (pale body, darker extremities), an Oriental Shorthair can be almost any color or pattern. Solid black, tabby, bicolor, smoke, ticked; the CFA recognizes the Oriental in well over 300 color and pattern combinations.[4]
Temperamentally, the two breeds are as close as any two officially separate breeds get. Both are loud. Both are attached to their people. Both need stimulation and companionship. The Siamese is one of the foundation breeds of the Oriental, and there is ongoing interbreeding between the two in many breeding programs.
If you want the Siamese personality but don’t want a pointed coat; if you’d prefer a solid black cat or a tabby-patterned cat with the same social intensity; the Oriental Shorthair is the direct answer.
One note: the Oriental Shorthair exists in both shorthair and longhair varieties. The Oriental Longhair (sometimes called the Javanese in older classifications) is the non-pointed equivalent of the Balinese.

## Siamese vs domestic shorthair: the lifestyle comparison
The domestic shorthair (DSH) is not a breed; it’s the cat equivalent of a mixed-breed dog. It encompasses cats of no specific pedigree, which means enormous genetic diversity and, consequently, enormous variation in personality, build, and health profile.
That diversity is both the domestic shorthair’s strength and the source of its core difference from the Siamese. A domestic shorthair can be anything from a highly interactive, talkative lap cat to a semi-independent cat that greets you twice a day and otherwise pursues its own agenda. You don’t know which you’re getting until the personality establishes itself, usually around six to twelve months of age.
With a Siamese, you know. The personality is predictable in a way that breeds specifically selected for temperament over generations always are. You are getting a loud, bonded, demanding, deeply affectionate cat. That predictability is a feature for owners who specifically want that combination.
Domestic shorthairs are generally more robust from a health standpoint. Genetic diversity reduces the risk of breed-specific inherited conditions. A DSH from a shelter is also, in most cases, far less expensive to acquire; an adoption fee of $50–$150 versus $400–$1,200 for a Siamese kitten from a reputable breeder.[5]

Neither is the wrong answer; they’re different answers to different questions.

## Siamese vs Tonkinese: the middle ground
The Tonkinese was deliberately developed in the 1960s by crossing Siamese with Burmese. The goal was a cat that split the difference; the social warmth and engagement of both parent breeds, with a temperament sitting between the Siamese’s high intensity and the Burmese’s relative calm.
It largely worked. The Tonkinese is vocal, but not at Siamese volume. It is social and people-oriented, but it handles being alone slightly better than a Siamese. The body type is intermediate: more muscular than a Siamese, less compact than a Burmese. Breeders sometimes describe it as “the Goldilocks cat.”
The coat is distinctive. Tonkinese come in three patterns: pointed (like a Siamese), solid (like a Burmese), and mink; a blend unique to the Tonkinese, where the contrast between body and points is softer and more graduated than the sharp Siamese demarcation. The eye color is typically blue-green or aqua rather than the deep sapphire blue of the Siamese.[5]
Health is generally robust, partly due to the hybrid vigor that comes from crossing two distinct lines. That said, the Tonkinese can inherit health risks from both parent breeds, so some vigilance is warranted.

## FAQ
**Is a Balinese cat the same as a Siamese?**
Genetically and temperamentally, the Balinese is a long-haired Siamese; a natural mutation that was preserved and developed as a separate breed from the 1950s onward. The personality is effectively identical. The coat is semi-long and silky, requiring slightly more grooming.
**Which is louder; Siamese or Oriental Shorthair?**
They are comparable. Both breeds share the same foreign body type and the same highly vocal temperament. If you’ve lived with one, you know what to expect from the other. Most owners and breeders describe the volume and persistence as essentially the same.
**Is a Burmese a good alternative to a Siamese?**
Yes, for owners who specifically want to dial down the noise. The Burmese is similarly social, similarly bonded to its people, and similarly active. It is quieter and handles being alone moderately better. If the Siamese’s voice is your main concern, the Burmese addresses it without abandoning the social personality.
**What is the calmest breed similar to a Siamese?**
The Tonkinese sits closest to calm on the Siamese-adjacent spectrum; notably quieter than the Siamese or Oriental Shorthair while retaining a high degree of social engagement. The Burmese is also calmer. If you want a vocal-breed personality without the decibel level, either is worth considering.
