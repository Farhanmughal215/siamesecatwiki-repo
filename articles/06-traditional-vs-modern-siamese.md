# Traditional vs modern Siamese: what’s the difference?
*Same name, two very different cats**,** and a century of show-ring politics separating them.*
> **Quick answer.** The traditional Siamese cat has a rounded head, moderate body, and the conformation seen in Thailand for centuries. The modern (show-type) Siamese was selectively developed by American breeders from the 1950s onward, producing an elongated wedge-shaped head, very large ears, and a long tubular body. In 2007, TICA recognized the traditional type as a separate breed called the Thai cat.

## What is a traditional Siamese cat?
The traditional Siamese is the older type. It’s the cat that arrived in Britain in the 1880s[1] and lived in Thai royal households for centuries before that. The head is rounded and moderately wedge-shaped, sometimes described as “apple-headed,” though breeders who keep this type often consider that term imprecise. The muzzle has a slight stop (a gentle step down from forehead to nose). The ears are medium-large and proportional to the head. The body is moderate: muscular, well-balanced, and not extreme in any direction.
In Thailand, this type is simply called a Wichien Maat, the name that appears in the *Tamra Maew*, the ancient Thai cat poetry manuscript that documented the breed’s appearance and significance.[2] In the West, it has acquired various labels over the decades: old-style, classic, applehead, or, since 2007, Thai cat, which is now a formally recognized breed with TICA[3] and several European registries.
The traditional type looks like what most non-enthusiasts picture when they think “Siamese.” Cream or pale body, darker points on face, ears, legs, and tail, vivid blue eyes. Build like a cat, not like an art-school drawing of one.

## What is a modern Siamese cat?
The modern Siamese is the product of several decades of show-ring selection, primarily in the United States, that pushed the breed’s features toward the extreme. The defining characteristic is the head: a long, narrow wedge with no break from forehead to nose tip when viewed in profile. The ears are very large and wide-set, continuing the triangular lines of the head. The body is long, tubular, and angular. The legs are long and fine-boned. The tail is thin and whip-like.
This is the Siamese that wins at CFA shows today.[4] It’s also the type most commonly seen in professional cat photography, film, and advertising from the 1960s onward. Many people who grew up watching American television know this type, it’s the cat from *That Darn Cat!* (1965), the cat in the Lady and the Tramp Siamese pair.
Both types have the same coat and point coloring, seal, chocolate, blue, and lilac are found in traditional and modern cats alike.

## How did the split happen?
The divergence began after World War II, when the American cat fancy began rewarding increasingly extreme features in the show ring. Judges in the 1950s and 1960s favored longer heads, larger ears, and finer bodies. Breeders responded by selecting for those traits. Each generation produced cats a step further from the original Thai type.
British breeders resisted longer, the UK’s GCCF standard retained a somewhat more moderate conformation, but by the 1980s, the extreme wedge type dominated international shows.[5]
Traditional breeders didn’t disappear. They maintained the original type quietly, often outside the main show circuit, and networked through smaller registries and breed clubs. Their cats remained much closer to the historical Thai conformation.
The formal recognition came in 2007, when TICA granted the Thai cat separate breed status.[3] This wasn’t a new breed being invented, it was a registry finally acknowledging that two distinct types had been developing in parallel for decades. European registries including WCF and FIFe followed with their own Thai cat standards.[6]

The most reliable diagnostic, if you’re looking at a cat and trying to place it: view the head from the side. A traditional Siamese has a visible step down at the nose. A modern show Siamese has a perfectly straight line from skull to nose tip.

## Are their personalities different?
Both types are vocal, social, and intensely bonded to their people. That core personality is consistent across both lines and is driven by the same underlying genetics, not the skull shape.
Where breeders and long-time owners sometimes differ: some describe traditional Siamese as somewhat calmer and less hyperactive than their modern counterparts. The modern show type, selected for extreme physical expression over generations, may have also been selected for a more intense behavioral presentation. But this is anecdotal. No controlled studies have compared behavioral profiles between the two types, and individual variation within either type is enormous.[7]
If you want a quieter, less demanding cat, neither type guarantees that. Socialize well, meet the parents if you can, and choose based on individual temperament.

## Are there health differences between the types?
Both types share the genetic heritage of the Siamese breed, which means both carry elevated risk for hepatic amyloidosis (a protein-deposit disease affecting the liver) and hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM, a thickening of the heart muscle).[8] These conditions are not exclusive to the Siamese, but they appear at higher rates than in most breeds. Neither type is immune.
The modern wedge type raises an additional concern specific to its morphology. Extreme skull elongation, the same trait that produces the long, narrow head, can narrow the upper airway and alter dental alignment. The effect is mild compared to brachycephalic breeds (flat-faced cats like Persians and Scottish Folds), but it’s worth noting. Anecdotal reports from veterinarians include slightly higher rates of dental crowding in extreme wedge Siamese, though peer-reviewed data specifically on this population is limited.
If you’re acquiring a modern show-type Siamese, ask the breeder whether any cats in their lines have had airway or dental issues. If you’re acquiring any Siamese, ask about HCM screening and amyloidosis in the breeding lines.

The practical implication: if you want a traditional-type kitten registered as such, look for breeders who register with TICA as Thai cat breeders, or with WCF/FIFe. CFA-registered “Siamese” will be the modern wedge type.
If you’re not interested in showing and just want a companion cat with the traditional appearance, the registry distinction matters less. What matters is finding a breeder who maintains the traditional conformation intentionally, not one who simply hasn’t pushed the wedge type as far as the show circuit.

## FAQ
**Is a traditional Siamese the same as an applehead Siamese?**
Largely yes. “Applehead” is the informal term most commonly used in North America to describe the rounded-headed traditional type. Breeders who work with this type sometimes prefer “traditional” or “Thai” as more precise terms. The formal registry name, since 2007, is Thai cat.
**Can a traditional and modern Siamese be bred together?**
Biologically yes, but a breeder serious about either type will keep the lines separate. Mixing would undermine the distinct conformation of both. TICA registers them as separate breeds for this reason.
**Which type is healthier?**
Neither is definitively healthier. Both share Siamese-specific disease risk. The modern type has some additional morphological concerns, but these are mild and not universal. Health comes down more to breeder practices, specifically HCM screening and genetic testing, than to type.
**Are traditional Siamese rare?**
In the Western show world, yes. The modern wedge type dominates CFA competition. But traditional-type cats are common in Thailand and have a dedicated breeder community worldwide. Finding one takes more searching than finding a show-type kitten from a CFA-registered breeder.
**Do they have the same blue eyes?**
Yes. The vivid blue eye color is caused by the same temperature-sensitive pigmentation genetics in both types. It’s one of the most consistent traits across all Siamese.
