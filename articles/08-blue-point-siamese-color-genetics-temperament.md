# Blue point Siamese: color, genetics, and temperament
*The dilute cousin of the seal point**,** softer, subtler, and prized for exactly those qualities.*
> **Quick answer.** A blue point Siamese has a bluish-white to platinum body with cool slate-gray points on the face, ears, paws, and tail. This color is produced by the dilution gene (the d allele at the MLPH locus), which disperses melanin granules and transforms what would otherwise be near-black seal coloring into a soft, cool gray. Blue point is one of the four classic Siamese point colors and is considered the second most recognized after seal point.

## What does a blue point Siamese look like?
The body is bluish-white to platinum gray, noticeably cooler in tone than the creamy ivory of a seal point. Where a seal point reads warm, a blue point reads cool. Some owners describe the body color as almost silvery in certain light.
The points, face mask, ears, legs, and tail, are a medium slate blue. Not navy, not charcoal. The color sits somewhere between medium gray and a cool steel blue, and it holds a consistent temperature across the extremities. The nose leather is dark slate gray. Paw pads are dark slate gray, sometimes with a faint blue cast. Eyes are vivid blue, the same deep sapphire as every classic Siamese point.
The distinction from seal point comes down to temperature, literally and figuratively. Seal points have near-black extremities against a warmer ivory body. Blue points have medium slate-gray extremities against a cooler, lighter body. Side by side, the difference is immediately clear. When you’re looking at just one cat, the easiest tells are the nose leather (dark brown-black in seal, slate gray in blue) and the body color (warmer in seal, cooler in blue).

## What causes the blue point color?
Every Siamese, regardless of point color, carries the same temperature-sensitive Himalayan allele at the *TYR* gene, which encodes the enzyme tyrosinase.[1] That allele makes tyrosinase heat-sensitive: at normal body temperature (above roughly 33 °C / 91 °F), the enzyme stops working and produces no pigment. In the cooler extremities, it functions normally, which is why color forms only on the points.
That mechanism produces the seal point by default. To get a blue point, you need a second gene on top: the dilution gene.[5]
The dilution gene is a recessive allele (*d*) at the MLPH locus, which encodes a protein called Melanophilin. Melanophilin is responsible for anchoring melanin granules inside pigment cells. When a cat inherits two copies of the *d* allele (one from each parent), the granules clump together instead of distributing evenly.[5] The result: what would have been dense near-black eumelanin in a seal point becomes diffuse, spread-out, and visually gray-blue.
A blue point is genetically a seal point with dilution added. The underlying eumelanin is the same black-brown pigment; the dilution gene simply changes how it’s arranged in the hair shaft.[2]
To produce a blue point kitten, both parents must carry at least one copy of the *d* allele. If both parents are seal points with no dilute ancestry, they cannot produce a blue point offspring.

## When did CFA recognize the blue point?
The CFA, the Cat Fanciers’ Association, the largest cat registry in North America, recognized the Siamese as a breed in 1906, and seal point was the founding color.[3] Blue point achieved CFA recognition in 1934.[3]
That gap matters. For nearly three decades, only seal points could compete as recognized Siamese at CFA shows. Blue points were bred and kept, but they existed outside the official standard. The 1934 recognition formalized what breeders had been producing for years.
Chocolate and lilac came later still. This sequence, seal, then blue, then the other two, reflects how breeders understood the genetics at the time. Seal was the obvious base. Blue, as the dilute of seal, was the next logical step. Chocolate and lilac took longer to understand as genetically distinct categories rather than variations of the same gene.[4]
TICA and most international registries now recognize all four classic point colors on equal footing.[4]

## How does the blue point color develop?
Blue point Siamese kittens are born almost completely white. For the first few days, there’s little to distinguish them from kittens of any other point color.
By one to two weeks, faint slate-gray ghosting appears on the ears and along the edges of the face. It’s subtle, less obvious than the shadow that appears on a darkening seal point, because the color itself is lighter. By weeks four to six, the points are distinct. Full adult color sets by 12 to 14 months, though the points may continue to deepen slightly through the cat’s second year.
This slightly slower visual development compared to seal point is simply a function of pigment density. Lighter colors show less contrast against a pale body, so early development is harder to see. The underlying biology, the timing of tyrosinase activation as extremities cool, is the same across all four classic colors.[1]
Like all Siamese, blue points can darken as they age, particularly in cooler climates. This happens because the whole body cools slightly with age, expanding the zone where tyrosinase stays active.

## Blue vs seal vs chocolate vs lilac: what sets them apart?
The four classic Siamese point colors are produced by two genes working in combination: the B locus (which controls whether pigment is black or brown) and the D locus (which controls whether that pigment is full-density or diluted). ## Are blue point Siamese calmer than other colors?
This is one of the more persistent claims in the Siamese owner community, and it deserves a direct answer: there is no peer-reviewed evidence that coat color correlates with temperament in Siamese cats.
All four classic point colors are the same breed. They share the same genetic background for behavior, the vocality, the social intensity, the curiosity, the need for interaction that defines the Siamese temperament.[6] The CFA and TICA breed standards describe a single temperament profile that applies to all Siamese regardless of point color.[3][4]
The “calmer blue point” idea may come from selection bias in informal breeder communities, or from the way visual subtlety creates an impression of quiet. If a cat is less visually striking, the eye may read it as more restrained. That’s aesthetics, not genetics.
What is true: individual cats within any breed vary considerably in temperament, and a calm blue point or a boisterous blue point can both exist. Breeder line, early socialization, and individual personality matter far more than point color.

## Frequently asked questions
**Is the blue point the rarest of the four classic Siamese colors?**
Blue is the second-most common after seal, though exact registration figures by color point are not consistently published by CFA. Chocolate and lilac are generally considered rarer, partly because their genetics require both parents to carry recessive alleles at the B locus.
**Are blue point Siamese kittens born white?**
Yes. All Siamese kittens are born white or near-white, regardless of point color. The temperature-sensitive mechanism that produces pigmentation needs cooler body zones to activate, and the womb provides uniform warmth. Points begin appearing within the first two weeks of life.[1]
**Can two seal point parents produce a blue point kitten?**
Yes, if both parents carry a hidden copy of the dilution allele (*d*). Because *d* is recessive, two seal points can both be carriers (D/d genotype) without showing any blue color themselves. A kitten that inherits *d* from both parents will be blue point.
**What’s the difference between a blue point and a lilac point?**
Both involve the dilution gene. The difference is the base pigment. Blue point is diluted black eumelanin (from the seal base). Lilac point is diluted brown eumelanin (from the chocolate base). Lilac points have pinkish-gray points and a glacial white body; blue points have slate-gray points and a bluish-white body.
**Do blue points get darker as they age?**
Somewhat. Like all Siamese, blue points can deepen slightly over time as the cat’s body temperature drops incrementally with age, expanding the zone where tyrosinase is active. The effect is less dramatic than in seal points because the pigment is already diluted.
