# Rare Siamese colors: lynx point, flame point, tortie, and cream
*Beyond the four CFA classics, Siamese genetics can produce striped, orange, tortoiseshell, and cream points — recognized by some registries, contested by others.*
> **Quick answer.** The rare Siamese colors — lynx point (tabby-striped points), flame point (orange points), tortie point (patchwork orange and base color), and cream point (pale diluted orange) — exist because additional genes interact with the standard Siamese pointed pattern. The Cat Fanciers’ Association (CFA) only recognizes seal, chocolate, blue, and lilac as Siamese. TICA and the GCCF recognize a broader range, often registering these cats as Siamese outright.

## Why does CFA only recognize four Siamese colors?
The short answer is history and breed philosophy.
When the Cat Fanciers’ Association established its Siamese breed standard, four point colors were the documented norm: seal, chocolate, blue, and lilac. Those four are produced by combinations of just two gene loci — the B locus (black vs chocolate) and the D locus (full color vs diluted). No additional genes involved, no ambiguity about breed origin.
The rare Siamese colors covered in this article require different genes: the tabby-agouti gene, the sex-linked orange gene, or both. Introducing those genes into a Siamese breeding program historically meant outcrossing — and CFA drew a line. Any pointed shorthair cat with a color outside the four classics would be registered as a Colorpoint Shorthair, not a Siamese.[1]
The Colorpoint Shorthair is its own CFA breed. But it is genetically and physically identical to a Siamese. Same body type, same pointed pattern, same blue eyes, same ancestry. It is a registration category, not a different animal.
TICA took a different position. In the late twentieth century, TICA merged its Colorpoint Shorthair registration into the Siamese breed, recognizing a far wider color range under a single breed name.[2] The UK’s GCCF similarly recognizes red (flame), tortie, tabby (lynx), and cream points as Siamese.[3]
For pet owners, the distinction rarely matters. For breeders aiming at CFA shows, it matters a great deal.

## What is a lynx point Siamese?
A lynx point Siamese — called tabby point in GCCF registrations — is a Siamese where the points show tabby striping instead of solid color.[3] The base pointed pattern is intact: the cat’s body is pale and the extremities are darker. But within those darker points, clear tabby markings are visible.
**What you see: **An “M” mark on the forehead. Rings on the legs and tail. Broken stripes on the face around the eyes and cheeks. The effect is a cat that looks like a faint tabby compressed into just the point areas, while the body stays almost white.
Every classic base color can produce a lynx point Siamese variant: seal lynx (dark brownish-black tabby stripes), blue lynx (gray-blue stripes), chocolate lynx (warm brown stripes), lilac lynx (pale frosty stripes with a pinkish tint), and flame lynx (orange tabby stripes, requiring the orange gene as well).
**The genetics: **The tabby pattern is controlled by the agouti locus (A locus). The dominant A allele allows tabby banding; the recessive a allele produces solid color. A cat with at least one A allele will show tabby patterning where pigment is present.[4] Because the Siamese’s pointed pattern restricts pigment to the extremities, the tabby banding appears only on those extremities — the body stays pale regardless.
Lynx points are sometimes described as having “ghost striping,” which disappears in bright light and reappears at closer angles. Many owners find the combined pointed-tabby effect striking precisely because of its subtlety.

## What is a flame point Siamese?
Flame point is the name used by most North American breeders. GCCF and some international registries call it red point. Either way: the points on a flame point Siamese are orange to deep red-orange, and the body is creamy white.[3]
**The genetics: **The orange color comes from the O gene on the X chromosome. It is sex-linked, which means males need only one copy (XᵒY) to be fully orange-pointed, while females need two copies (XᵒXᵒ) for a non-tortie flame point expression. A female with one copy (XᵒX) becomes a tortie point instead — orange and base color together in patches.[5]
The O gene does not produce a separate pigment. It redirects the pigment-producing pathway: instead of making eumelanin (black/brown), the cells produce phaeomelanin (orange/red). In a flame point Siamese, this redirection happens across all the point areas, producing vivid orange on the face, ears, legs, and tail.
**Registry status: **TICA recognizes flame point as Siamese. GCCF recognizes red point as Siamese. CFA registers these cats as Colorpoint Shorthair — same physical standard, different breed name in that registry.[1][2]

## What is a tortie point Siamese?
Tortie points are female-only, with rare exceptions.
The tortoiseshell pattern requires two different versions of the X-linked orange gene on two different X chromosomes: one chromosome carrying O (orange) and one carrying the non-orange allele (o). Because males have only one X chromosome, they can only be one or the other — not both at once. Females with XᵒXo express both, and the result is a mosaic of orange patches and base-color patches distributed across the coat.[5]
In a tortie point Siamese, that mosaic is confined to the points. The body stays pale. The face, ears, legs, and tail show irregular patches of orange mixed with whatever the base color is. A seal tortie point has patches of orange and near-black; a blue tortie (blue-cream) shows orange and blue-gray; a chocolate tortie shows orange and warm brown; a lilac tortie (lilac-cream) shows diluted orange (cream) and pinkish-gray.
The patching is random. No two tortie point Siamese cats have identical markings. The patches can be large blocks or tiny flecks, and the distribution is unpredictable.
Male tortie Siamese exist, but only as a genetic anomaly — usually Klinefelter’s syndrome (XXY), giving them two X chromosomes. These cats are nearly always sterile and are quite rare.[4]

## What is a cream point Siamese?
Cream point is the diluted version of flame point. The same O gene redirects pigment production to phaeomelanin (orange), and then the dilution gene (d allele, homozygous dd) softens it from bright orange to a pale buff-cream.[4]
**What you see: **Points that are a pale, warm cream — not quite orange, not quite beige. The body is very white. The overall impression is extremely pale, even softer than lilac point in terms of contrast between body and points.
Like flame point, cream point expression in females requires two copies of the O gene. A female with one O allele will be a dilute tortie (blue-cream) rather than a solid cream point.
Cream point is recognized by TICA and GCCF as a Siamese color. CFA registers cream point cats as Colorpoint Shorthair.[1][2][3]

The practical upshot: a flame point Siamese purchased from a TICA-registered breeder is, in that system, a Siamese. The same cat in a CFA context would compete as a Colorpoint Shorthair. The cat does not know the difference, and neither does its personality or health.

## FAQ
**Are Colorpoint Shorthairs really Siamese?**
Genetically and physically, yes. The CFA created the Colorpoint Shorthair as a separate breed registration to accommodate Siamese-type cats with point colors outside the four classics, without officially expanding the Siamese breed standard. TICA resolved this by merging the two registrations. The cats are the same animal; the split is administrative.[1][2]
**Can two seal point parents produce a flame point kitten?**
No. The flame point Siamese requires the O (orange) gene, which is sex-linked and must come from at least one parent. Two seal points with no orange ancestry cannot carry or produce the O gene. A flame point kitten requires at least one parent who carries the O allele — typically a tortie or flame/red point parent.[5]
**Are lynx points rarer than classic points?**
In registered Siamese populations, yes — lynx point Siamese are less common than the four classic solid-point colors, partly because introducing the tabby agouti gene requires specific breeding choices. Among the broader cat population, lynx point coloring is not especially unusual. CFA does not publish disaggregated color-point registration data.
**Does the registry category matter for pet owners?**
For most pet owners, no. Whether a flame point is registered as Siamese or Colorpoint Shorthair has no bearing on the cat’s health, temperament, or care needs. It matters primarily for breeders entering cats in CFA conformation shows, where the breed and color class determine competition eligibility.
