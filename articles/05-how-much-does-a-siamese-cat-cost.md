# How much does a Siamese cat cost?
*Prices vary more than most people expect**;** here’s how to read what you’re actually paying for.*
> **Quick answer.** Siamese cat prices typically range from $75–$200 for a rescue adoption to $400–$2,000 for a kitten from a reputable breeder, with show-quality cats at the higher end. The difference comes down to pedigree, health testing, and the breeder’s registration status. Annual ownership costs run roughly $1,150–$2,600 once food, vet care, and insurance are included.

## What does a Siamese kitten cost from a breeder?
The honest answer is: it depends on three things; pedigree, location, and what health testing the breeder does.
A pet-quality Siamese kitten from a reputable, registered breeder typically falls in the **$400–$1,000 range** in the United States. “Pet quality” doesn’t mean inferior; it means the kitten has some trait (a slightly heavier body, a coat pattern not ideal for the show ring) that puts it outside strict show competition. These cats make perfectly good companions.
Show-quality kittens; meaning those whose conformation closely matches the breed standard set by CFA[1] or TICA[2]; typically start around **$1,200 and can reach $2,000 or more**, particularly from breeders with a strong competition record or from lines carrying specific colors.
**Note: ***Kitten prices are not published by the registries, and they shift with demand and regional economics. The ranges above are compiled from breeder listings and community data as of early 2026. Verify current prices directly with breeders in your area.*
Several things push the price up:
- ***Color rarity. ******Lilac point and chocolate point Siamese are rarer than seal and blue point, and some breeders price accordingly.***
- ***Breeder location. ******Breeders in high cost-of-living cities tend to charge more than rural breeders.***
- ***Health testing. ******A breeder who screens for HCM (hypertrophic cardiomyopathy) and hereditary amyloidosis***;*** two conditions with elevated rates in Siamese[3]***;*** invests real money in that testing. It shows in the price, and it’s worth it.***
- ***Included vet work. ******Responsible breeders send kittens out vaccinated, microchipped, and sometimes spayed or neutered. That work has a cost.***

## Is adopting a Siamese from a rescue cheaper?
Yes, significantly. Siamese-specific rescues and general cat rescues typically charge **$75–$200** in adoption fees. That fee usually covers initial vet care, vaccines, spay/neuter, and microchip; services that would cost you $300–$500 if you arranged them separately.
Rescue Siamese are often adults. That’s a feature, not a bug. An adult cat’s personality is fully formed; you know what you’re getting. Kittens are a gamble on temperament; a three-year-old Siamese is not.
Siamese are surrendered to rescues fairly often. The breed’s vocal, demanding personality is wonderful if you’re prepared for it and overwhelming if you’re not. Rescues frequently have Siamese or Siamese mixes available. Organizations like Siamese Rescue[4] (US-based) maintain waiting lists and adoption processes worth exploring.

## What are the ongoing costs of owning a Siamese?
The kitten price is a one-time number. The ongoing costs are what you actually need to budget for.
Siamese are active, social cats with specific needs. They do poorly with isolation, thrive on interaction, and benefit from enrichment; all of which has cost implications. They also carry elevated risk for two health conditions (amyloidosis and HCM[3]) that make pet insurance worth considering more seriously than with some other breeds.

Pet insurance deserves a specific mention. HCM in Siamese; a thickening of the heart wall; can require ongoing cardiology care. Amyloidosis, a protein-deposit disease affecting the liver and kidneys, is expensive to manage. Neither is inevitable, but the risk is real enough that insurance math makes sense before your cat is diagnosed, not after.[3]

## Where should you not buy a Siamese cat?
Three places to avoid:
**Pet stores. **Most pet store kittens come from commercial breeding operations (often called kitten mills) that prioritize volume over health. These cats frequently arrive with respiratory infections, parasites, or early signs of genetic disease. You’re also paying retail markup on top of all of it.
**Online classified listings and social media. **“Siamese kittens for sale” posts on Facebook Marketplace or Craigslist may occasionally be legitimate, but they’re also a common front for mill-sourced kittens. There’s no registry accountability, no health testing, and no recourse if the kitten gets sick two weeks after you bring it home.
**Breeders who won’t let you visit. **A reputable breeder invites you to see where the cats live. If someone insists on meeting at a neutral location or shipping the kitten without a visit, that’s a sign the breeding conditions aren’t something they want you to see.
The short-term “savings” from a cheap kitten often evaporate in the first year of vet bills.

## How to spot a reputable breeder
A reputable Siamese breeder does several things consistently.
**Registration. **They register their cats and litters with CFA[1], TICA[2], or another recognized registry. This creates accountability; registered breeders can be reported and suspended.
**Health testing. **At minimum, breeding cats should be screened for HCM (via echocardiogram) and ideally tested for genetic markers associated with Siamese-specific conditions. Ask to see test results; a good breeder will have them ready.[6]
**They ask you questions. **A breeder who doesn’t ask about your home, living situation, or experience with cats isn’t being selective. Selectivity is a good sign.
**Written contract. **You should receive a written sales contract that specifies what happens if the cat develops a genetic condition within a defined window. Some breeders offer refunds; others offer replacement. Either way, get it in writing.
**Limited litters. **Breeders who always have kittens available, or who breed multiple species simultaneously, are production operations more than breeding programs. Look for someone focused on one or two breeds with deliberate litter timing.
The CFA breeder referral list and TICA’s breeder directory are both reasonable starting points.[1][2]

## FAQ
**Is a $400 Siamese kitten worse than a $1,500 one?**
Not necessarily. Price reflects pedigree strength and show potential more than companion quality. A $400 kitten from a health-tested, registered breeder can be as healthy and personable as a $1,500 show prospect. What matters most is the breeder’s practices, not the number on the invoice.
**Do Siamese cats cost more to care for than other breeds?**
Slightly, on average; mainly because of their elevated health risks and their need for enrichment and social interaction. A bored, lonely Siamese can develop stress-related behaviors that lead to vet visits. Budget for engagement, not just food and litter.
**Are male or female Siamese cats more expensive?**
Some breeders charge more for females, particularly those sold with breeding rights. Pet-quality males and females typically run similar prices from the same litter.
**What’s included in a typical adoption fee from a rescue?**
Most rescues include spay/neuter, core vaccines, microchip, and a vet health check in the adoption fee. Some also include FeLV/FIV testing. Confirm with the rescue; what’s included varies by organization.
