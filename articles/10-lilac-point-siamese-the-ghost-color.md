# Lilac point Siamese: the ghost color
*The palest of the four classic points**,** and the only one that needs two separate dilution genes to exist.*
> **Quick answer.** A lilac point Siamese has a glacial white body and pinkish-gray points, face, ears, paws, and tail. The color is also called frost point. It is the rarest of the four CFA-recognized Siamese point colors because a cat must inherit both the chocolate gene and the dilution gene from each parent, a double-recessive combination that is statistically the least likely of the four classics.

## What does a lilac point Siamese look like?
The lilac point is the softest, most muted of the four classic Siamese colors. Where the seal is dark and dramatic, lilac is almost translucent.
**Body: **Glacial white, sometimes called magnolia or off-white. The body should have no cream or yellow cast, just a cool, near-pure white. Kittens look almost blank at first glance.
**Points: **The face mask, ears, legs, and tail are a frosty gray with a distinct pinkish or lavender tint. The technical description from the CFA breed standard calls for “frost gray with pinkish tone.”[1] That pink is the key. It is not blue-gray, not charcoal, not beige, it is a warm, dusty pinkish-lavender that reads differently in every light.
**Nose leather and paw pads: **Both are lavender-pink. This is the single most reliable way to distinguish a lilac point from a blue point. The nose leather is the tell.
**Eyes: **Deep vivid blue, the same as all Siamese. Eye color does not vary between point colors.
The overall impression is delicate. Show breeders sometimes describe lilac points as looking like the cat is lit from within, the coloring is that pale. In bright sunlight, the pinkish cast in the points is unmistakable. In low light, lilac and blue points are easy to confuse, which is why the nose leather check matters.

## Lilac vs blue point Siamese: how do you tell them apart?
These two are the most commonly confused Siamese colors, and the confusion is understandable. Both have pale points on a nearly white body. Both are dilute colors. But they are genetically distinct and visually distinguishable once you know what to look at.

The fastest field test: look at the nose leather. A blue point has a nose that reads definitively gray. A lilac point has a nose leather with a pink or lavender tone. Under fluorescent light it can be subtle; in natural daylight it is clear.
The body color also helps. Blue points have a body with a blue-white or cold-white cast. Lilac points are truly glacial, the body reads warmer and whiter simultaneously.
If you have both cats in the room together, the difference is immediately obvious. Seen individually, especially in photos, the confusion is real and common.

## What genetics produce the lilac point Siamese color?
Every Siamese carries a temperature-sensitive variant of the *TYR* gene, which means pigment forms only in the cooler extremities.[2] That explains the pointed pattern shared by all four classic colors. What separates lilac from the others comes down to two separate genetic loci working together.
**The B locus (black/chocolate): **The B gene controls whether black pigment (eumelanin) is fully expressed or reduced to brown. The dominant B allele produces black/seal pigment. The recessive b allele shifts black to chocolate brown. A cat needs two copies of b (bb genotype) to express chocolate or lilac coloring.[3]
**The D locus (dilution): **The D gene controls whether pigment granules clump tightly (full, saturated color) or spread out loosely (diluted, softer color). The dominant D allele gives full color. The recessive d allele dilutes it. Two copies (dd) are required for dilution to show.[3]
Here is how the four classics fall on this 2×2 grid:

**Full color (DD or Dd)**
**Diluted (dd)**
**Seal/black (BB or Bb)**
**Seal point**
**Blue point**
**Chocolate (bb)**
**Chocolate point**
**Lilac point ← double recessive**

Lilac occupies the bottom-right corner of that grid. It requires bb (chocolate) and dd (dilution) simultaneously, both recessives expressed at once. That is the double-recessive combination.
A cat’s parents can both look like seal points and still produce a lilac kitten, as long as each parent secretly carries one b allele and one d allele. Those carriers show no lilac coloring themselves, but when their recessive alleles meet in the same kitten, lilac emerges.[3]

## Why is the lilac point Siamese rarer than the other three?
Rarity comes down to carrier frequency. To produce a lilac point kitten, both parents must carry at least one copy of the b allele (for chocolate) and at least one copy of the d allele (for dilution). Two separate recessive alleles, both needing to be present in both parents at the same time.
Seal point is the most common because it requires neither b nor d, it is the default expression of the pointed gene. Blue requires only d. Chocolate requires only b. Lilac requires both.
In practical terms: even if you pair two cats who both carry b and both carry d, each kitten has only a 1-in-4 chance of inheriting two b alleles, and independently a 1-in-4 chance of inheriting two d alleles. The probability of both happening at once in the same kitten is roughly 1 in 16, assuming the parents carry both recessives in the first place.[4]
In the broader cat population, the b allele (chocolate) is rarer than the d allele (dilution). Fewer cats carry the chocolate recessive than the dilution recessive. That scarcity in the breeding pool pushes lilac frequency lower still.

## When did the CFA recognize the lilac point?
The Siamese Cat Society of America showed the first Siamese in Britain in the 1890s, but the breed standard at that time recognized only seal point. Chocolate and blue points were accepted into the CFA Siamese breed standard through the mid-twentieth century as breeders documented the genetics and established consistent type.[1]
Lilac and chocolate points took longer to achieve recognition than seal and blue, partly because they required the b allele, which had to be deliberately introduced and stabilized in the breeding population. The CFA now recognizes all four classic points (seal, chocolate, blue, and lilac) as Siamese.[1] TICA and the UK’s GCCF also recognize lilac point in the Siamese breed.[5][6]
Some breeders and registries use the term “frost point” interchangeably with lilac point, particularly in North American breeder communities. The CFA standard uses “lilac.” GCCF uses “lilac” as well.

## FAQ
**Is lilac point the same as frost point?**
Yes. Frost point is an informal name for the lilac point Siamese, used by some North American breeders. Both terms describe the same color: frosty pinkish-gray points on a glacial white body. The CFA breed standard uses “lilac.”
**Can two seal point parents produce a lilac kitten?**
They can, if both parents carry one b allele (for chocolate) and one d allele (for dilution) silently. Seal point cats can carry these recessives without showing any sign of them. When two carriers are paired, a lilac kitten is statistically possible, though far from guaranteed. A DNA test for b and d alleles can identify carriers before breeding.[3]
**Do lilac points darken with age like other Siamese?**
Yes. All Siamese, including lilac points, darken gradually as they age. The body temperature drops slightly in older cats, allowing the temperature-sensitive tyrosinase enzyme to produce pigment in areas that were previously too warm. A lilac point at ten years old will have noticeably darker points than the same cat at one year old.
**Are lilac points a separate breed from other Siamese?**
No. Lilac point is a color, not a breed. A lilac point Siamese is the same breed as a seal point, chocolate point, or blue point Siamese, the same body type, temperament, and genetic background, with one color variant. CFA registers all four as a single breed.
