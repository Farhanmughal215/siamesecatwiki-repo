# Amyloidosis in Siamese cats: what owners need to know
*Amyloidosis is a hereditary condition that causes abnormal proteins to accumulate in the liver. Siamese cats are one of the breeds most likely to develop it.*
> **Quick answer.** Amyloidosis in Siamese cats is a hereditary disease where a misfolded protein called amyloid deposits in the liver, progressively destroying liver tissue. Siamese and Abyssinian cats are the breeds most commonly affected. The condition often progresses silently until significant liver damage has occurred, and prognosis is generally poor once symptoms appear. Regular bloodwork is the best early-warning tool for owners.

## What is amyloidosis in cats?
Amyloid is not one specific protein. It is a structural form that proteins can take when they fold incorrectly. When a protein misfolds, instead of doing its job and being recycled, it aggregates into rigid, insoluble fibers. Those fibers accumulate in organ tissue, and the body cannot break them down.
In cats, amyloid most often deposits in the liver (hepatic amyloidosis) or the kidneys (renal amyloidosis), depending on the breed and the type of amyloid protein involved. The deposits physically displace healthy liver cells and interfere with normal liver architecture. Over time, enough tissue is replaced that the liver can no longer perform its basic functions: filtering toxins, producing proteins, and regulating blood sugar.
The form seen in Siamese cats is predominantly hepatic. The liver is the primary target. Because the liver is highly vascular, amyloid deposition can weaken its tissue to the point of spontaneous rupture, which is a life-threatening emergency.[1]

## Why are Siamese cats prone to amyloidosis?
The predisposition in Siamese cats appears to be hereditary. Studies going back to the 1980s established that the condition clusters in Siamese families, pointing to a genetic basis rather than random occurrence.[2][3]
The exact inheritance pattern is not fully characterized. A recessive mode of inheritance has been proposed, meaning a cat would need to inherit a faulty copy of the relevant gene from both parents to develop the disease, but this has not been confirmed with enough precision to allow a commercial genetic screening test.[4]
Siamese and Abyssinian cats are the two breeds most consistently reported with amyloidosis, but they develop different forms. The amyloidosis in Abyssinians tends to target the kidneys (renal amyloidosis). In Siamese, the liver is the primary affected organ. Both breeds can develop amyloidosis in multiple organs, but the hepatic pattern dominates in Siamese clinical reports.[1][3]
Because the genetic cause is not fully defined, there is no available screening test to determine whether a kitten carries the predisposition. Regular health monitoring, not genetic clearance, is the primary tool for Siamese owners.

## Siamese cat amyloidosis symptoms: what to watch for
Amyloidosis progresses in stages, and the early stages are easily missed. The liver has considerable functional reserve; it can lose a substantial portion of its capacity before outward signs appear.
**Early signs (often subtle, easy to attribute to other causes):  **Gradual weight loss, reduced appetite, mild lethargy or decreased activity.
**Mid-stage signs (as liver function declines):  **Vomiting (sometimes persistent), jaundice (yellowing of the skin, gums, and whites of the eyes), increased drinking and urination, and distended abdomen from fluid accumulation (ascites).
**Late-stage and acute presentations:  **Sudden collapse or acute abdomen. This can occur when amyloid deposition has weakened the liver enough that it ruptures, causing internal bleeding into the abdominal cavity. This is a medical emergency and carries very high mortality.[1]

## How is amyloidosis diagnosed in Siamese cats?
### Liver biopsy
A liver biopsy is the definitive diagnostic tool. The tissue sample is stained with Congo red dye; amyloid deposits appear orange-red under normal light and show a characteristic apple-green birefringence under polarized light microscopy.[5] This appearance is diagnostic. No other tissue change looks the same.
Biopsy carries some procedural risk, particularly in cats with advanced disease where the liver may be fragile. Your vet will weigh the benefit of a definitive diagnosis against the risks of the procedure.
### Ultrasound
Abdominal ultrasound can show liver changes consistent with amyloidosis: altered echogenicity, irregular texture, changes in size, and free abdominal fluid. It is not diagnostic on its own, but it guides the workup and helps assess disease extent.
### Blood panels
A liver panel will typically show elevated liver enzymes (ALT, AST, ALP), elevated bilirubin in jaundiced cats, and low albumin because a damaged liver produces less. These findings indicate liver disease but do not specify the cause. Ultrasound findings and blood panel abnormalities together make amyloidosis a differential diagnosis. Confirmation requires biopsy.[5]

## What is the prognosis for a Siamese cat with amyloidosis?
Prognosis for cats with symptomatic amyloidosis is generally poor. The liver damage caused by amyloid deposits is irreversible. Affected tissue does not regenerate the way mild liver inflammation might resolve with treatment.
Some cats with early or mid-stage disease can be stabilized with supportive care and maintained at reasonable quality of life for months. Cats that have progressed to significant liver failure, or that present with hepatic rupture, face a much grimmer short-term outcome.
The most important prognostic factor is how much functional liver tissue remains at the time of diagnosis. This is why proactive monitoring, detecting abnormalities before clinical signs appear, gives the best chance of a longer and more comfortable course.

## How is Siamese cat amyloidosis managed?
There is no cure for amyloidosis in cats. The amyloid deposits cannot be removed. Management focuses on supporting liver function, slowing progression where possible, and maintaining quality of life.
Supportive care typically includes:[6]
- ***Prescription liver diet: ******reduced protein diets reduce the liver's workload; modified fat content supports digestion***
- ***Fluid therapy: ******intravenous or subcutaneous fluids support hydration and help with toxin clearance***
- ***Anti-nausea and appetite-stimulating medications: ******to manage vomiting and maintain caloric intake***
- ***Management of ascites: ******if fluid accumulation is significant, drainage (abdominocentesis) may provide relief***
- ***Colchicine: ******has been used in cats with hepatic amyloidosis based on evidence in human medicine and limited veterinary case reports, though controlled trial evidence in cats is limited[6]***

All treatment decisions should be made by your vet based on your cat's bloodwork, imaging findings, and clinical condition. Do not administer any medication without veterinary guidance.

## What Siamese cat owners can do proactively
Because early amyloidosis produces subtle or no signs, proactive monitoring is the most effective tool available.
- ***Annual bloodwork from age 5. ******Request a full chemistry panel including liver enzymes (ALT, ALP, AST), bilirubin, albumin, and total protein. Trends matter: a value that rises year over year is more informative than any single reading.[4]***
- ***Weigh your cat monthly. ******Weight loss in cats is often gradual and easy to miss on a kitchen scale. A loss of even 200 to 300 grams over a few months without a dietary explanation is worth raising with your vet.***
- ***Take early vomiting seriously. ******A new pattern of regular vomiting, especially with weight loss or reduced appetite, deserves investigation rather than a wait-and-see approach.***
- ***Discuss breeding history. ******If you acquired your Siamese from a breeder, ask whether amyloidosis has appeared in the lines. This does not clear a cat of risk, but it provides context.***

## Siamese cat amyloidosis: frequently asked questions
**Is amyloidosis in Siamese cats always fatal?**
It is a serious, progressive disease with no cure, but it is not always immediately fatal. Some cats live for months to over a year with supportive care after diagnosis. The key variables are how much liver function remains and how well the cat responds to management. Sudden death from hepatic rupture is a real risk in advanced disease, but not every cat follows that path.
**Can I test my Siamese kitten for amyloidosis?**
There is no validated genetic test for amyloidosis in Siamese cats. The hereditary basis is established but the specific gene has not been characterized to the point of a commercial test. Annual bloodwork as the cat ages is the practical alternative to genetic screening.
**How is hepatic amyloidosis different from renal amyloidosis?**
Both involve amyloid deposits, but in different organs. Hepatic amyloidosis (liver) is the primary form in Siamese. Renal amyloidosis (kidneys) is the primary form in Abyssinians, though Siamese can develop it too. Kidney-targeted amyloidosis causes increased thirst, urination, and eventually kidney failure rather than the liver signs described in this article.
**What is the difference between amyloidosis and hepatic lipidosis in cats?**
Hepatic lipidosis occurs when fat accumulates in liver cells, usually triggered by a period of not eating. It is potentially reversible with aggressive nutritional support. Amyloidosis involves irreversible protein deposits and carries a generally worse long-term prognosis. Your vet can distinguish between them through bloodwork, imaging, and biopsy if needed.
