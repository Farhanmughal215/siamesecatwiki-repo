# The History of the Siamese Cat: Origins, Timeline & Breed Evolution
*From the courts of Ayutthaya to the Crystal Palace cat show — the documented story of one of the world's oldest named cat breeds.*
> **Quick answer.** The Siamese cat originated in Thailand, then called Siam, where a pale-bodied, dark-pointed cat called the Wichianmat is described in the Tamra Maew — illustrated manuscripts from the Ayutthaya period. The breed reached the United States in 1878 and Britain in 1884, becoming one of the first cat breeds formally exhibited in the Western show ring.
## Where Do Siamese Cats Come From?
The Siamese cat comes from Thailand. The name Siamese comes from Siam, the Western name for Thailand until 1939. In Thai, the breed is called the Wichianmat, translated roughly as moon diamond or white gem.[1]
The clearest early documentation is the Tamra Maew (Cat-Book Poems), illustrated manuscripts produced in the Ayutthaya Kingdom (1351-1767 AD). These manuscripts describe dozens of cat types, assigning each an auspicious or inauspicious significance. The Wichianmat — pale body, dark face, ears, paws, and tail — appears among the cats described as bringing good fortune to their owners.[2]
The Tamra Maew are court documents, not scientific records. What they confirm is that a cat matching the Siamese description was known, valued, and considered distinctive enough to document — probably several centuries before Western contact.
One caveat: the oldest surviving Tamra Maew manuscripts date from the Rattanakosin period (after 1782), even though the text tradition is widely believed to be older. The breed's Thai history is real; the manuscript dates require careful framing.[2][3]
## What Does the Tamra Maew Say About the Siamese Cat?
The Tamra Maew describes the Wichianmat in terms of appearance and fortune. The cat is depicted with a cream or white body, darker coloring at the extremities, and light-colored eyes. The text classifies it as auspicious — a cat that brings good luck and prosperity.[2]
What the manuscripts do not say: that the Wichianmat was restricted to royalty, that keeping one outside the palace was forbidden, or that harming one carried any penalty. The association with aristocracy comes from the documents' court context, not from any explicit royal decree.[3]
## When Did Siamese Cats First Reach the United States?
Seven years before the famous British cat show, and largely forgotten by history.
In 1878, David Stickels — the American consul in Bangkok — sent a Siamese cat to the White House as a gift for President Rutherford B. Hayes and his wife Lucy. The cat was named Siam. The White House Historical Association records this as the first documented Siamese cat in the United States.[4]
Siam did not spark a breeding program. The gift was a diplomatic curiosity. The line left no lasting trace in the American cat fancy. The true American chapter of Siamese cat history began later, from British-imported stock.
## When Did Siamese Cats First Arrive in Britain?
Owen Gould, British consul-general to Bangkok, received a pair of Siamese cats as a farewell gift when he left Bangkok in 1884. He brought the cats to England that year.[1][5]
His sister Lilian Gould then exhibited them at the Crystal Palace National Cat Club show in London in January 1885.[5][6] Harrison Weir, who organized Britain's first structured cat shows, described the cats in his 1889 book Our Cats and All About Them as causing considerable sensation — visitors were reportedly startled by the animals' unusual appearance.[6]
The Crystal Palace show was a turning point. Within a few years, Siamese cats were actively sought by British cat enthusiasts. The first British breed club — the Siamese Cat Club — was established in 1902.[1]

## How Did the Siamese Cat Develop in the United States?
American interest in Siamese cats grew from British imports, not the forgotten Hayes cat. From the late 1880s onward, British Siamese breeders exported cats to American enthusiasts. The Cat Fanciers' Association (CFA) recorded its first registered Siamese in 1906.[1]
Early show Siamese on both sides of the Atlantic looked nothing like the cats winning championships today. They were moderate in build — rounded heads, medium ears, well-muscled bodies. The type was closer to what TICA now recognizes as the Thai cat than to the extreme wedge-head show Siamese of the present.
## How Did the Siamese Breed Standard Change Over Time?
The divergence between traditional and modern Siamese is the defining story of the breed in the twentieth century.
From the 1950s onward, American show judges began favoring increasingly extreme features: longer heads, wider ears, finer bones, more tubular bodies. Breeders responded to what the judges rewarded. Each generation of show cats moved further from the original Thai type. British breeders followed more slowly, but by the 1980s, the elongated wedge-head dominated international competition.[7]
Traditional breeders — maintaining the original rounded-head, moderate-body conformation — continued working outside the main show circuit. Their cats were sometimes called applehead, old-style, or classic.
The formal resolution came in 2007, when TICA granted the traditional type separate breed status under the name Thai cat.[7] WCF and FIFe followed with their own Thai cat standards. The CFA has not recognized the Thai cat as separate; its Siamese standard describes the modern wedge type.

## Where Does the Siamese Cat Stand Today?
The Siamese remains one of the most recognized cat breeds in the world, and one of the oldest with documented history in both Eastern and Western sources.
In the show world, the modern wedge-type Siamese continues to compete under that name in CFA and most international registries. TICA registers both the modern Siamese and the traditional Thai cat. The split is administrative, not biological — the two types share the same ancestry, the same four classic point colors, and the same fundamental temperament.
In Thailand, cats resembling the historical Wichianmat type are still common. The country where the breed originated does not classify its street cats by Western registry standards, but the appearance of the original type persists in the population.
## Frequently Asked Questions
**Where do Siamese cats originally come from?**
Thailand, historically called Siam by Westerners. The breed is documented in the Tamra Maew, a collection of Thai court manuscripts from the Ayutthaya period. The Thai name for the breed is Wichianmat.
**When did Siamese cats come to the West?**
The first documented Siamese in the United States arrived in 1878, sent to President Rutherford B. Hayes by the American consul in Bangkok. Britain received its first Siamese in 1884, when Owen Gould brought a breeding pair from Bangkok. The cats were publicly exhibited at the Crystal Palace show in January 1885.
**Were Siamese cats really royal cats in Thailand?**
The claim that Siamese cats were exclusively royal property is not supported by primary historical documents. The Tamra Maew associates the Wichianmat with good fortune and aristocratic households but contains no language of royal exclusivity or legal penalties for ownership. The royal cat story was amplified by Western press after the 1885 exhibition.
**Why do modern show Siamese look so different from old photographs?**
Because the Western show standard changed substantially from the 1950s onward. American breeders and judges progressively selected for an elongated head, large ears, and a very fine body — quite different from the moderate, rounded cats that arrived in Britain in the 1880s. TICA recognized the original type as a separate breed (the Thai cat) in 2007.
**What is the Tamra Maew?**
The Tamra Maew, or Cat-Book Poems, is a collection of illustrated Thai manuscripts from the Ayutthaya period that describes and classifies cat types by their markings and assigns each an auspicious or inauspicious meaning. It is the earliest known documentary record of the Siamese cat type.
