# Siamese.wiki — Writer's Handover Guide

Everything a writer needs to produce publishable articles for siamese.wiki. Voice, structure, SEO rules, the café-link policy, and a full worked example outline for Article #1.

---

## 1 · What siamese.wiki is

An independent, editorially serious wiki about the Siamese cat breed. It is **sponsored** by Siamese Cat Café in Bangna, Bangkok, but it is not a café blog. The wiki's job is to be the best free English-language reference on Siamese cats, full stop.

> **The audience:** prospective Siamese owners, current owners with a specific question, and curious cat enthusiasts. Mostly from search. They land, scan, get the answer, maybe read more. They did not come to hear about our café — but if they're in Bangkok, we want them to know we exist.

---

## 2 · Voice & tone

### Write like this

- **Warm but precise.** Like a well-read friend who happens to be a vet tech. Human, never clinical, never syrupy.
- **Confident.** Take positions. "The seal point is the darkest of the four classic points" — not "some sources say the seal point might be darker."
- **Specific.** Numbers, dates, places, sources. Not "a long time ago" — "in 1884, in a London fog."
- **Short sentences win.** Especially at the top. Readers are scanning.
- **Plain English.** If you use a technical term (tyrosinase, amyloidosis), define it in-line.

### Good vs avoid

**✅ Good:**
> Siamese cats darken with age. That's the short answer. The longer one involves a temperature-sensitive enzyme called tyrosinase, which only produces pigment in the cat's cooler body parts — face, ears, paws, tail.

**❌ Avoid:**
> Siamese cats, which are a very popular breed of feline, often undergo various color changes throughout their lives. This is a fascinating phenomenon that many people find interesting…

### Things we never do

- "Dear reader," "in today's article," "let's dive in," "the purr-fect…" — all banned.
- No breed mythmaking presented as fact. Legends are fine, but flagged as legends.
- No affiliate links. No "best Siamese food on Amazon" posts. We recommend products only when genuinely useful and never with an affiliate tag.
- No medical advice. We describe conditions; we always say "see a vet" for anything serious.
- No content lifted from siamesecat.cafe. The wiki never writes about *our* cats by name.

## 3 · Structure every article follows

1. **H1 title** — keyword-forward, question-style when it fits, ~60 chars max
2. **Deck** — 1 sentence beneath H1, summarizing the payoff
3. **Quick answer box** — 2–3 sentences, optimized for Google's featured snippet
4. **Hero image** — 16:9, with a caption that adds information, not just labels
5. **Body** — H2-sectioned, each H2 answers one reader question, ~200–400 words per section
6. **Comparison / table / diagram** — at least one scannable visual element
7. **Inline café callout** — placed at a natural breath in the article (never in a health article's opening)
8. **FAQ block** — 3–5 questions at the bottom, each with a 1–3 sentence answer (fuels Google's People-Also-Ask)
9. **"Keep reading" links** — 3 related wiki articles
10. **Sources** — numbered list at the very bottom

---

## 4 · The quick-answer box (most important block on the page)

Google often reads the first 40–60 words after the H1 and quotes them as a "featured snippet." That's free top-of-page real estate. Every article needs a box that looks like this:

> **QUICK ANSWER.** A seal point Siamese is a cat with an ivory body and near-black (seal brown) extremities — face, ears, paws, tail. The color is caused by a temperature-sensitive gene that only lets pigment form in the cat's cooler body zones. Seal is the darkest and most common of the four classic Siamese point colors.

**Rules for this box:**

- 40–60 words
- Answers the exact keyword question directly, in the first sentence
- No pronouns pointing to earlier text (it stands alone if quoted)
- No hype words, no "discover," no "in this article"

## 5 · The café-link policy

This is the whole reason the wiki exists. Get it right.

| Article type | CTA intensity | What to include |
|---|---|---|
| Personality, voice, colors, behavior, "meet a Siamese" | **LOUD** | Inline card mid-article + sticky footer + floating widget |
| General breed guides, history, comparisons | **MEDIUM** | Inline card + footer map block |
| Health, medical, serious conditions | **SUBTLE** | Footer-only mention. Never an inline CTA inside a health topic. |

> **The golden rule:** every CTA goes to one of exactly two destinations — the **Google Maps pin** (`https://maps.app.goo.gl/o2Z7yN1yRLNKScro8`) or the **café homepage** (`https://siamesecat.cafe/`). No deep links into the café site's cat profiles, menu, or blog. We do not want search engines to treat the two sites as overlapping.

### CTA copy to rotate through

- "Want to meet one in person? 16 rescued Siamese live at our café in Bangna, Bangkok — free entry, every day."
- "In Bangkok? Meet real Siamese cats at our sponsor café — opens Google Maps →"
- "Sponsored: Siamese Cat Café, Bangna. Free entry, 8 AM – 9 PM daily."

## 6 · SEO rules you must follow

- **One keyword per article** (the primary), plus 2–3 semantic variants. Don't try to rank two articles on the same keyword.
- **Keyword in:** H1, quick-answer box's first sentence, one H2, first image's alt text, meta description.
- **Never keyword-stuff.** If the keyword doesn't flow, rewrite the sentence.
- **Internal links:** every article links to 3–5 other wiki articles. Use descriptive anchor text ("seal point Siamese"), never "click here."
- **External links:** cite sources with real links. Vet journals, CFA/TICA, Wikipedia, peer-reviewed studies. Open in new tab.
- **Meta description:** 150–160 chars, includes primary keyword, reads like a sentence a human wrote.
- **Images:** WebP format, under 200KB, every image has alt text describing what's shown (not keyword-stuffed).

---

## 7 · Fact-checking & sourcing

Every non-trivial claim needs a source. Acceptable sources, in order of trust:

1. Peer-reviewed veterinary journals (e.g. *Journal of Feline Medicine and Surgery*)
2. Cat registries: CFA, TICA, GCCF, WCF breed standards
3. University vet schools (Cornell, UC Davis, RVC)
4. Reputable general references: Wikipedia with good citations, *International Cat Care*
5. Established breeder or rescue organizations

**Not acceptable:** Reddit threads, Quora, random pet blogs, AI-generated sites, anonymous forum posts.

---

## 8 · Legal & compliance

- **No veterinary advice.** Replace "treat this with…" with "speak to your vet about…"
- **No breed claims we can't source.** "Siamese are the most intelligent breed" → "Siamese are widely regarded as highly intelligent; no single objective measure ranks cat breeds by IQ."
- **No stolen images.** Use Unsplash, Pexels, Wikimedia Commons with attribution, or license from a stock site. Keep a credits line at the bottom of each article.
- **AI disclosure:** If an article is AI-drafted, a human editor must review and be credited as the reviewer. The byline is the reviewer, not the drafter.

### Draft of the opening (to set the tone)

---

**Siamese cat breed guide: history, types & personality**

*Everything you need to know about the oldest recognized Asian cat breed — its history, colors, personality, and what makes it the loudest, chattiest cat you'll ever meet.*

> **Quick answer.** The Siamese is a short-haired cat breed from Thailand, historically called Wichianmat. It is best known for its pointed coat pattern — a pale body with dark face, ears, paws and tail — deep blue eyes, and an unusually loud voice. There are two types today: the traditional (applehead) and the modern show Siamese (wedgehead).

Most cat breeds have a vague origin story. The Siamese has a date.

In 1884, the British consul-general to Siam received a pair of cats as a farewell gift. His sister Lilian exhibited them at London's Crystal Palace cat show. Within a decade, the Siamese was the most fashionable cat in England. Within a century, it had been remodelled twice, split into two lineages, and become one of the most recognizable cats on earth.

This guide covers where it came from, what it looks like, how it behaves, and whether it's the right cat for you. If it is — there are a lot of them looking for homes.

## 10 · Submission checklist

Before you mark an article as "ready for review":

- [ ] H1 contains the primary keyword
- [ ] Quick-answer box is 40–60 words and self-contained
- [ ] Every H2 is a question a reader would actually type
- [ ] At least one table or diagram
- [ ] 3–5 internal wiki links with descriptive anchors
- [ ] 1 café CTA placed correctly for the article's tier
- [ ] FAQ section with 3–5 Q&As
- [ ] Sources list, numbered, with working links
- [ ] All images have alt text, are under 200KB, and are WebP
- [ ] Meta title (≤60 chars) and meta description (≤160 chars) written
- [ ] Read the article out loud. Fix anything that sounds like an AI wrote it.

---

> **Editor's final filter:** "Would I send this article to a friend who just asked me this exact question?" If yes, ship it. If not, one more pass.
